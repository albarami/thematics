## Theme 1: Elderly wellbeing as dignified integrated balance, spiritual-moral steadiness, and lived acceptance

### Definition
Day 4 participants define elderly wellbeing as an integrated condition rather than a narrow biomedical state. Across Arabic and English material, older-age wellbeing is described through balance across the pillars, peace of mind, acceptance of reality, emotional steadiness, spiritual orientation, and the preservation of dignity under bodily change and dependency risk. The distinct Day 4 contribution is that older-age wellbeing is rarely treated as simple happiness or simple health. Participants repeatedly describe it as a dignified way of living within ageing rather than denying it.

### Key questions
- **Primary**: Q1-Q3
- **Secondary**: Q4, where the same ideal becomes a benchmark for judging current services

### Key participants and sources
- `D4_P26`, `D4_P29` (`HWEL10AR`) — English-language holistic, spiritual, and multidimensional formulations of elderly wellbeing
- `D4_P01`, `D4_P03`, `D4_P04`, `D4_P05` (`HWEL1AR`) — acceptance, رضا, faith, and balance under realistic life limits
- `D4_P08`, `D4_P12` (`HWEL3AR`) — peace of mind, capability, rights, and adaptive wellbeing across the life course
- `D4_P14`, `D4_P16` (`HWEL7AR`) — 360-degree elder assessment and integrated social-psychological-spiritual framing
- `D4_P22`, `D4_P23`, `D4_P24`, `D4_P25` (`HWEL9AR`) — daily self-balancing, peace of mind, safety, and expectation-sensitive accounts of wellbeing

### Tensions within this theme
- Participants describe wellbeing as integrated, yet they often weight spiritual and dignity-related elements more heavily than a flat “five equal pillars” model would suggest
- Acceptance is central, but it is not passive surrender; several speakers combine رضا with effort, adaptation, and self-maintenance
- Medical stability is repeatedly treated as insufficient on its own if psychological, social, or dignity-related conditions are missing

### Supporting codes
`balance_multidimensional`, `contentment_acceptance`, `spiritual_moral_anchor`, `dignity_autonomy`, `inner_peace_stability`, `safety_security`, `mental_health_elderly`

## Theme 2: Good ageing as relational belonging, non-burden, and continued social significance

### Definition
Day 4 does not frame ageing well as a purely individual achievement. Participants repeatedly describe older people as needing to remain heard, valued, socially located, and meaningfully connected to family and community rather than reduced to dependency, passivity, or burden. This theme becomes strongest when speakers move between respect language (`كبار القدر`), emotional holding, intergenerational recognition, and the fear that needing help may erase older people’s place in family or social life.

### Key questions
- **Primary**: Q1-Q4
- **Secondary**: Q5, where the same relational strain reappears as a challenge in care delivery

### Key participants and sources
- `D4_P27`, `D4_P28`, `D4_P29` (`HWEL10AR`) — non-burden, hidden struggle, and family-linked accounts of older-age wellbeing
- `D4_P05` (`HWEL1AR`) — productivity, movement, and not socially retiring people too early
- `D4_P08`, `D4_P10` (`HWEL3AR`) — older people as influential family members rather than passive dependants
- `D4_P13`, `D4_P15`, `D4_P17` (`HWEL7AR`) — dignity, containment, respect, and anti-burden framing in service and recipient speech
- `D4_P22`, `D4_P25` (`HWEL9AR`) — family ecology, expectation, and social-role implications of ageing

### Tensions within this theme
- Family appears as a core support ecology, but several segments also show how family can undermine autonomy, fail to communicate, or make care emotionally thin
- Older people may need help, yet many participants insist that good care should not translate into over-helping or symbolic erasure of competence
- Respectful language matters, but several Day 4 segments imply that naming dignity is easier than operationalising it consistently in everyday interaction

### Supporting codes
`family_caregiver_ecology`, `dependence_resistance`, `dignity_autonomy`, `community_support_programs`, `age_dignity_language`, `post_retirement_gap`, `intergenerational_shift`, `containment_emotional_holding`

## Theme 3: Holistic elder care as partially present but unevenly integrated across services, training, and coordination

### Definition
Participants do not describe the current Day 4 service landscape as wholly deficient or wholly successful. Instead, they describe real examples of holistic elder-sensitive care - home care, rehabilitation settings, multidisciplinary work, I-COPE, Ihsan, and other institutional examples - alongside repeated insistence that these examples are not yet a stable system-wide norm. In practice, holistic elderly care often depends on unit-specific models, motivated individuals, or specific provider teams while time pressure, training gaps, cultural mismatch, and weak coordination continue to limit consistent delivery.

### Key questions
- **Primary**: Q4-Q6
- **Secondary**: Q5, where the same service-structure issues are named directly as obstacles

### Key participants and sources
- `D4_P26`, `D4_P27`, `D4_P28` (`HWEL10AR`) — individual-doctor dependence, MDT examples, I-COPE, time pressure, and language barriers
- `D4_P02` (`HWEL1AR`) — role-sensitive detection, referral/tracking gaps, and psychologically informed medication conversations
- `D4_P12` (`HWEL3AR`) — service reflection, implementation gaps, and provider-grounded care interpretation
- `D4_P13`, `D4_P14`, `D4_P17`, `D4_P20`, `D4_P21` (`HWEL7AR`) — home care, rehabilitation, institutional inconsistency, silos, and non-institutional reliance on personal commitment
- `D4_P23`, `D4_P25` (`HWEL9AR`) — elder-service examples, gaps in implementation, and recipient-facing reflections on service trust and adequacy

### Tensions within this theme
- Participants name high-quality existing services, yet many insist those services remain patchy rather than fully institutionalised
- Holistic care is described as desirable and sometimes visible, but the strongest critiques show how staffing, coordination, and communication conditions still reward fragmented practice
- Providers often describe person-centred intent, but service-recipient material shows that intention does not always translate into felt inclusion or clear support

### Supporting codes
`service_adequacy_reflection`, `institutional_example`, `workforce_constraint`, `care_fragmentation`, `training_deficit`, `cultural_barrier`, `awareness_deficit`, `institutional_culture_gap`, `multidisciplinary_team`, `home_care_services`, `person_centered_care`, `rehabilitation_services`

## Theme 4: Institutionally embedded and socially distributed routes to elder-care redesign

### Definition
When Day 4 participants move from critique into future-oriented proposals, they do not rely on generic calls to “improve services.” Their strongest redesign logic combines institutional embedding, public awareness, training, communication standards, differentiated pathways, home-care and community models, clearer policy expectations, inter-institutional partnership, and family/community-facing outreach. The central Day 4 logic is that elderly wellbeing will not be embedded in healthcare through goodwill alone. It must be designed into systems, learning pathways, public communication, and everyday service organisation.

### Key questions
- **Primary**: Q5-Q7
- **Secondary**: none — the redesign material is concentrated in the late challenge, opportunity, and recommendation sections

### Key participants and sources
- `D4_P26`, `D4_P28`, `D4_P29` (`HWEL10AR`) — clinic expansion, awareness, school curriculum, policy clarity, translator provision, and communication training
- `D4_P08`, `D4_P09`, `D4_P10`, `D4_P11`, `D4_P12` (`HWEL3AR`) — technology, training, annual assessment, specialist preparation, and elderly-club/community models
- `D4_P14`, `D4_P17`, `D4_P20`, `D4_P21` (`HWEL7AR`) — institutional culture, formal embedding, social/media outreach, and organisational follow-through
- `D4_P22`, `D4_P23`, `D4_P25` (`HWEL9AR`) — community partnership, public awareness, mental-health de-stigmatisation, and professional-development conditions tied to service redesign

### Tensions within this theme
- Participants want formal institutional change, but much of the same evidence base shows how uneven training and siloed work currently block implementation
- Technology is often proposed as support, yet several segments insist it should not replace judgement, relational care, or appropriate timing of intervention
- Public awareness and community partnership are recurrent redesign routes, but the Day 4 material also shows persistent trust, communication, and access gaps that such efforts would need to overcome

### Supporting codes
`practical_suggestion`, `awareness_education`, `technology_integration`, `policy_recommendation`, `training_development`, `interdisciplinary_model`, `community_support_programs`, `institutional_example`, `home_care_services`

## Cross-cutting pattern: The five pillars are treated as weighted rather than flat in older age

### Description
Across Day 4, participants rarely treat the five pillars as a neutral checklist of equal parts. Spiritual orientation, social connection, dignity, respect, and emotional steadiness are often described as the elements that organise, guide, or stabilise the others. This pattern strengthens Theme 1 by complicating any flat reading of multidimensional wellbeing, strengthens Theme 2 by showing why belonging and non-burden matter so strongly in older age, and strengthens Theme 3 by showing why services that focus only on physical or procedural care are repeatedly judged as insufficient.

### Evidence status
- Present across `HWEL1AR`, `HWEL7AR`, and `HWEL9AR`, with partial support in `HWEL10AR`
- Retained as a cross-cutting analytical pattern rather than a standalone final theme
- Interpreted cautiously because some of the strongest support comes from explicit pillar-ranking talk in Q3 rather than from every source equally
