# CASE_D4 Candidate Themes

## Case: CASE_D4 — Day 4 (Elderly)
## Candidate count: 6 candidate themes tested
## Scope status: Gate 4 theme layer only. Gate 1-3 are complete, Gate 4 is the current scope, and CASE_D4 is still not complete, not package-ready, and not yet at the Day 4 report layer.

---

## Method note

The Day 4 candidate-theme set was developed from close reading of the promoted `CASE_D4_coded_segments.csv` base, with code clusters used only for retrieval, traceability, and evidence organization. Moderator rows remained contextual only and were excluded from participant evidence, quotation diversity, and theme support. The Day 4 recommendation workbook was not used to generate themes. No `note_taker_summary` rows were operationalized in this Gate 4 evidence layer.

## Candidate 1: Elderly wellbeing as dignified integrated balance, spiritual-moral steadiness, and lived acceptance

### Core idea
Day 4 repeatedly defines older-age wellbeing as more than symptom control or physical function. Participants across Arabic and English sources describe wellbeing through balance across the five pillars, peace of mind, acceptance of life as it is, faith, emotional steadiness, and dignified selfhood under the realities of ageing.

### Why it held as a candidate
- Strong Q1-Q3 spread across all five transcript sources
- Present in both professional and service-recipient positioned material
- Repeatedly anchored in Arabic moral-spiritual language as well as English holistic language

### Decision
**Retained as Final Theme 1** because the conceptual definition of wellbeing is one of the strongest and broadest structures in the Day 4 data.

---

## Candidate 2: Good ageing as relational belonging, non-burden, and continued social significance

### Core idea
Participants do not describe older people as isolated individuals whose wellbeing can be understood apart from family, community, and social recognition. Good ageing is repeatedly framed through being heard, respected, included, not reduced to dependency, and not treated as a burden or expired life-stage.

### Why it held as a candidate
- Strong support across Q1-Q4, with later echoes in Q5
- Distinctive Day 4 language around `كبار القدر`, containment, family support, and anti-burden framing
- Supported by both provider and patient/service-user voices

### Decision
**Retained as Final Theme 2** because the relational and dignity-loaded framing of ageing is analytically distinct from the more conceptual wellbeing definition in Candidate 1.

---

## Candidate 3: The five pillars are weighted rather than flat in older age

### Core idea
Although the five pillars are discussed as a framework, participants often refuse to treat them as equal. Spiritual orientation, social connection, dignity, and respect are repeatedly described as organising or stabilising the others rather than sitting beside them as neutral categories.

### Why it held as a candidate
- Strongest in Q3 discussions of pillar prioritisation
- Appears in Arabic material especially clearly, but also echoes in English discussion of hidden burden, isolation, and integration
- Sharpens the interpretation of both wellbeing and service-reflection material

### Decision
**Absorbed as a cross-cutting analytical pattern, not retained as a standalone final theme.** It strengthens Final Themes 1-3, but does not operate as an independent Day 4 meaning-system separate from them.

---

## Candidate 4: Holistic elder care is partially present but uneven, person-dependent, and not yet fully institutionalised

### Core idea
Day 4 participants identify real examples of holistic and elderly-sensitive care - home care, rehabilitation, MDT structures, I-COPE, Ihsan, and fast-tracking or specialist services - but repeatedly insist that these examples do not yet add up to a stable institution-wide model. Holistic practice often depends on individual provider orientation, specific units, or local initiatives.

### Why it held as a candidate
- Strong Q4-Q6 spread
- Supported by clear named institutional examples plus explicit service-critique material
- Holds both positive and negative evidence without collapsing either side

### Decision
**Retained as Final Theme 3** because it captures the central Day 4 mismatch between the ideal of holistic elder care and its uneven institutional reality.

---

## Candidate 5: Elder-care redesign should be built through institutional embedding, public awareness, differentiated services, and community/home-based routes

### Core idea
When participants move from critique into possibilities, they do not speak only in vague reform language. They propose practical institutional embedding, awareness work, training, communication standards, school and public education, elderly clubs, home-care expansion, differentiated pathways, inter-institutional partnerships, and more formal incorporation of the five-pillar logic into healthcare practice.

### Why it held as a candidate
- Strongest in Q5-Q7
- Spans English and Arabic sources
- Includes both policy/system and community/family-facing redesign routes

### Decision
**Retained as Final Theme 4** because the redesign material is rich, multi-source, and coherent enough to stand as a distinct late-question implementation theme.

---

## Candidate 6: Technology, precision health, and innovation should stand as a separate future-facing theme

### Core idea
Some Day 4 participants discuss digital tools, apps, media, smart medication reminders, and even epigenetics/precision-health language as opportunities for improving elderly care and wellbeing.

### Why it held as a candidate
- Appears especially in English Table 10 and some late Q6/Q7 Arabic material
- Carries strong future-facing language and practical optimism
- Could initially look like a distinct innovation theme

### Decision
**Absorbed into Final Theme 4 rather than retained as a standalone final theme.** The innovation material is real, but it is narrower in speaker spread and works better as one redesign route among several rather than as an independent Day 4 theme.

---

## Final selection logic

- Four final themes were retained because each has clear multi-speaker, multi-question, and multi-source support.
- One additional candidate was retained only as a cross-cutting analytical pattern because it sharpens several themes rather than standing alone.
- One narrower candidate was absorbed into the redesign theme because it did not have enough independent breadth to justify standalone status.
- No candidate theme was built from moderator material, note-taker material, or the auxiliary recommendation workbook.
