# Full Project Methodology

## Purpose
This document sets out the methodology for the full thematic analysis project.

The methodology covers the full analytic design and explains how the corpus was defined, how sources were treated, how the analysis was conducted within and across cases, and how transparency and methodological consistency were maintained throughout the project.

## Current assessed scope
The current active assessment scope covers CASE_D1 through CASE_D4 only. All foundation outputs, inventories, registers, and case-level analysis work are bounded to D1-D4. The master design below describes the broader project framework; any references to cases beyond D1-D4 apply only if and when the scope is formally expanded.

## Study design
The project uses a reflexive thematic analysis design applied to a multi-case qualitative dataset.

The dataset is organised into day-based stakeholder cases. The current assessed scope includes:

- `CASE_D1` - Day 1 Childhood
- `CASE_D2` - Day 2 Youth
- `CASE_D3` - Day 3 Adults
- `CASE_D4` - Day 4 Elderly

Each day is treated as a distinct stakeholder case. The methodology therefore combines two linked levels of analysis:

1. a within-case analysis for each day
2. a later cross-case comparative analysis across the assessed cases

This design preserves the integrity of each stakeholder discussion before any broader comparison is attempted.

## Methodological orientation
The project uses a reflexive thematic analysis approach.

This means the analysis focuses on patterned meaning rather than on mechanical counting. Coding and theme development are interpretive, iterative, and theory-informed at the level of analytic judgement, while remaining grounded in the source material. Themes are developed through active analytic engagement with the data rather than extracted as if they were self-evident categories.

Keyword or pattern matching may be used as a retrieval aid, indexing aid, traceability aid, and support layer for organising evidence, but it is not the primary analytic engine. A segment may support a theme even if it does not contain an expected keyword, and a keyword hit does not by itself establish thematic support.

Theme development is therefore driven primarily by reflexive close reading, contextual interpretation, memo-based reasoning, speaker-role distinction, question-context reading, tension and contradiction analysis, and culturally and linguistically informed interpretation, especially in Arabic and mixed-language material.

The approach is case-sensitive. It treats stakeholder discussions as contextually situated accounts and aims to understand how wellbeing, support, services, barriers, and opportunities are constructed within each case before examining similarities and differences across cases.

## Corpus and case structure
The corpus consists of materials located under the day folders in the parent workspace. The current assessed scope covers:

- `Day1_Childhood`
- `Day2_Youth`
- `Day3_Adults`
- `Day4_Elderly`

Within the methodology, each day folder functions as one case. Within each case, each document is treated as a distinct source item with its own provenance.

Following the corpus refresh, the current corpus version includes transcript files, note-taker files, and structured recommendation workbooks for Days 1-4. All inventoried files are retained in the documentation layer, but they do not all carry the same analytic weight.

### Primary analytic unit
The primary analytic unit is the case.

### Within-case source unit
The source unit is the individual document within a case.

### Coding unit
The coding unit is a flexible meaning unit. Meaning units vary in length according to interpretive relevance rather than fixed sentence, line, or paragraph boundaries.

## Data sources and source roles
The methodology distinguishes clearly between source types.

### Primary verbal data
Files located in `Audio Recordings _Transcripts` are treated as the primary verbal data for analysis. These materials carry the main weight of interpretation because they preserve participant talk most directly.

### Contextual and triangulation data
Files located in `NoteTakers_Notes` are treated as contextual and triangulation data. These sources are used to clarify context, support interpretation, identify convergence or divergence, and flag issues requiring caution. They do not replace transcript-based interpretation.

### Treatment of Structured Recommendation Sheets and Post-Workshop Summary Artifacts
Files located in `Recommendations_Data` are treated as auxiliary structured recommendation data rather than as transcript-equivalent primary evidence.

The methodology distinguishes three source layers clearly:

- primary verbal data: transcript files that preserve participant talk most directly
- note-taker/contextual data: note files that support context, triangulation, and caution
- auxiliary structured recommendation data: post-workshop or workshop-generated recommendation sheets that organize suggestions in a cleaned or grouped form

Structured recommendation sheets may be used to support recommendation-focused reporting, especially for action-oriented discussion prompts such as Q6 and Q7, or to populate recommendation summary tables. They do not drive theme generation, they are not treated as direct participant quotations unless row-level traceability is explicitly demonstrated, and they must be labeled clearly as structured recommendation material. If a sheet contains grouped, cleaned, or post-discussion synthesis rather than raw workshop speech, that status must be stated explicitly in the methodology and case-level reporting.

### Auxiliary material
Files outside these two core source categories are treated as auxiliary material unless they are explicitly incorporated through a documented methodological decision.

### Temporary system files
Temporary lock files or system-generated artifacts are inventoried for transparency but excluded from analytic interpretation unless substantive content is demonstrated.

## Inclusion, exclusion, and corpus boundaries
The methodology is non-destructive and boundary-conscious.

Raw files remain in their original locations and are not renamed, overwritten, or edited. Derived analytic materials are created only inside the analysis workspace. Corpus inclusion and exclusion decisions are documented so that the chain of evidence remains visible from source material to later outputs.

Case boundaries are maintained during within-case analysis. Material from one stakeholder case is not used to shape the coding or thematic interpretation of another case during the initial case-level analysis. Cross-case interpretation occurs only after the separate case analyses have been developed in their own right.

## Corpus Amendment and Reanalysis Rule
When the corpus changes after an earlier inventory or case analysis has already been completed, the raw day folders must be rescanned and the inventory, case register, pairing assumptions, and source-role decisions must be refreshed before new analytic work proceeds.

If a case receives materially expanded core data such as additional transcripts or additional note-taker files, the case analysis must be rerun from the beginning rather than patched selectively. Earlier reports, matrices, participant sheets, theme files, and related outputs remain archived for audit and provenance purposes, but they are treated as superseded rather than current. Final reporting must be based on the latest approved corpus version and the outputs regenerated from that version after any last-round corrections.

Auxiliary-only additions do not automatically require a full rerun, but they do require an explicit methodological decision about whether they affect reporting, recommendation synthesis, or evidential framing. Structured recommendation sheets therefore trigger review of reporting logic even when they do not count as primary theme-generating evidence.

## Data management and reproducibility
The project is organised as a documented analysis workspace separate from the raw corpus.

This structure supports reproducibility in the procedural sense by preserving:

- separation between raw and derived materials
- file-level inventory and case registration
- corpus refresh reports where amendments occur
- processing logs for transformation steps
- audit-trail records for analytic decisions
- provenance links between sources and outputs
- versioned derived artifacts where relevant
- supersession notes where earlier case outputs are replaced by refreshed reruns

Because reflexive thematic analysis is interpretive, reproducibility is not treated as identical analyst replication of meanings. Instead, the methodology supports transparency, traceability, and defensible explanation of how interpretations were produced.

## Analytic procedure
The full analytic procedure is organised in linked stages.

### 1. Establish the analytic foundation
A protocol is used to define the corpus, case structure, source roles, documentation standards, provenance rules, and audit-trail requirements. This creates a stable methodological foundation before case-level interpretation is developed.

### 2. Prepare and organise the corpus
The corpus is inventoried and organised into an analysis workspace. Source files are registered by case, source type, and provenance. Any preparation or conversion decisions are documented so that later analysis remains traceable.

If the corpus is amended after an earlier pass, this stage is repeated as a formal refresh. The inventory, case register, source maps, and pairing assumptions are regenerated from the raw folders before any within-case rerun proceeds.

### 3. Familiarise within each case
Prepared materials are read and re-read within each stakeholder case to build familiarity with content, tone, recurring concerns, and source-specific differences. Early analytic attention is directed to patterned meanings, tensions, absences, emphases, and contextual signals without collapsing cases into a single pooled reading.

### 4. Preserve discussion context
Where discussion guides or question structures are available, they are used as contextual aids to preserve the sequence and movement of each discussion. Guide structure informs contextual reading but does not determine the themes.

### 5. Code meaning units within cases
Each case is segmented into coded meaning units. Coding is interpretive rather than purely descriptive. Some codes remain close to participant wording, while others capture a more conceptual or latent level of meaning.

The coding layer provides a structured support system for retrieval, comparison, traceability, and evidence organisation. It does not mechanically determine whether a segment supports a theme.

Codes are developed within each case before cross-case comparison. This preserves case integrity and reduces the risk that one stakeholder group's language will dominate the interpretation of another group's material.

### 6. Write reflexive and methodological memos
Memoing accompanies the analytic process. Reflexive and methodological memos are used to document decisions, assumptions, uncertainties, boundary issues, language concerns, source-sensitivity issues, and emerging interpretive questions.

Memoing functions as an analytic tool rather than a clerical record only. It supports deeper interpretation, tracks shifts in understanding, and makes the reasoning behind later theme development more visible.

### 7. Develop candidate themes within cases
Related codes and patterned meanings are grouped into candidate themes within each case. Candidate themes are reviewed for coherence, distinctiveness, explanatory usefulness, and evidential support across the relevant case materials.

This stage focuses on whether multiple segments express a broader shared meaning that matters for understanding the case, rather than whether they merely repeat the same topic label. Close reading, question context, speaker role, discussion flow, tensions, contradictions, and memoed interpretation are used to judge thematic fit. Arabic concepts and local cultural meanings are interpreted in context rather than reduced to literal term matching.

### 8. Refine final within-case themes
Candidate themes are refined into final case-level themes through iterative review of coded segments, excerpt banks, memos, contextual notes, question flow, speaker-role distinctions, and theme-development records. Boundaries between themes are tested carefully so that each final theme captures a distinct patterned meaning.

### 9. Conduct cross-case comparison
After within-case themes have been established, a cross-case comparative stage examines convergence, divergence, overlap, contrast, and stakeholder-specific emphasis across the assessed cases.

Cross-case comparison does not erase case differences. Its purpose is to compare how patterned meanings travel, change, intensify, or narrow across stakeholder groups while retaining sensitivity to the context in which each case was produced.

### 10. Produce final synthesis and reporting
The final reporting stage integrates within-case findings, cross-case comparison, methodological explanation, supporting appendices, structured matrices, participant summary sheets, theme evidence sheets, and descriptive visual outputs. Reporting remains anchored in the documented chain of evidence and distinguishes clearly between interpretation, supporting evidence, and any supplementary salience summaries.

Final reporting is therefore supported by a formal analytic reporting infrastructure rather than by narrative write-up alone. Question-level matrices, participant-level summaries, theme evidence workbooks, tables, and charts are used to organise evidence, strengthen quotation coverage, clarify distribution across questions and sources, and support transparency in how the final report is assembled. These outputs support retrieval, traceability, and evidential organisation; they do not generate themes by themselves. Where auxiliary structured recommendation sheets are used, their contribution must be explicitly labeled as recommendation-layer support rather than merged invisibly into transcript-grounded participant evidence.

## Treatment of language and translation
The corpus includes mixed-language material.

Original-language material remains authoritative throughout the analysis. English support is used selectively where needed for clarity in analytic documentation or reporting, but translated wording does not replace the source-language material as the primary basis of interpretation.

No translated derivative dataset is treated as a substitute corpus. Where language handling affects interpretation, this is documented explicitly in translation or methodological records.

## Reflexivity
Reflexivity is built into the methodology rather than treated as an optional add-on.

The analysis recognises that thematic development is shaped by analytic judgement. Reflexive practice is therefore used to examine assumptions, interpretive habits, decisions about emphasis, source weighting, category boundaries, and possible overreach in claims.

Reflexive memos are used to document these considerations across preparation, coding, theme development, and comparative synthesis. This helps keep the analysis self-aware, transparent, and methodologically disciplined.

## Salience and prevalence
The methodology allows for supplementary salience judgements, but these do not generate themes.

Any salience, prominence, prevalence, or weighting layer is applied only after themes have been developed through interpretive analysis. Its role is descriptive rather than determinative. It may help clarify which retained themes are broader, more concentrated, or more central within a case or across cases, but it does not establish thematic validity on its own.

This protects the analysis from becoming count-driven while still allowing transparent discussion of relative prominence where analytically useful.

## Visual analytic reporting layer
After within-case coding and theme development, the project generates visual and tabular outputs to clarify the data structure and support interpretation.

These outputs are supplementary analytic aids. They do not generate themes, replace reflexive thematic interpretation, or statistically validate the findings. Their purpose is to clarify distribution of themes across questions and sources, show relative prominence of themes, support transparency in reporting, and help readers understand how patterns are distributed within and across cases.

Visual and tabular outputs may include:

- question-by-theme matrices
- participant-by-question summary tables
- theme prominence tables
- case comparison tables
- charts showing relative theme prominence
- charts showing source spread across transcripts, notes, or both
- charts showing question coverage by theme

## Question-level analytic matrix procedure
Each discussion question will have its own analytic matrix.

For each question, the matrix records:

- question ID
- question wording
- main response patterns
- linked codes
- linked final theme or themes
- relative prominence within that question
- supporting participant voices
- notes on divergence or tension

This matrix is used to connect question, participant responses, coded patterns, and themes. It is a formal bridge between question structure and thematic interpretation, and it supports transparent reporting of how each question contributes to the final case analysis. Moderator prompts, facilitator probes, and discussion-guide wording are recorded only as contextual framing and are excluded from participant-voice fields, quotation counts, and theme-evidence counts.

## Participant-level summary procedure
Participant-level summary sheets will be created for each case where participant identity information or defensible role labels are available in the source material.

These sheets capture:

- participant identifier or role label
- case or day
- source file
- question or questions contributed to
- short summary of what the participant mainly said
- linked theme or themes
- notable quotations
- language notes where relevant

Participant summaries are used to increase quotation diversity, avoid over-reliance on only a few voices, support balanced reporting across participants, and improve the evidentiary basis of the final report. These summaries support reporting infrastructure only and do not replace transcript-based interpretation.

Moderator or facilitator speech is not entered as participant-summary content. If a line is uncertain or question-like, it is labelled as contextual rather than counted as participant evidence.

## Quotation-density and quotation-diversity rule
Final reporting will include stronger quotation support drawn from multiple participants rather than relying on a narrow set of examples.

Each major question section should include quotations from multiple participants where feasible. Each final theme should also be supported by quotations from more than one participant where the data allow. Quotations should be selected to show both convergence and tension rather than agreement alone.

Note-derived quotations must be clearly distinguished from transcript-derived quotations. Moderator prompts and discussion-guide wording must not be counted as participant quotations. Original-language quotations remain authoritative where relevant, with English support used only as needed for readability.

## Visual output procedure
Charts and graphs will be created only from the structured matrices and spreadsheets, not from undocumented impressionistic judgement.

The following visual outputs will be generated where appropriate:

- a bar chart of relative prominence of themes within each case
- a bar chart of theme prominence by question
- a table or heatmap of question-to-theme mapping
- a table or heatmap of source spread across themes
- a case comparison table after all within-case analyses are complete

These visuals are descriptive and explanatory only. They do not replace thematic interpretation and they do not statistically validate themes. Their function is to help readers see the structure of the findings more clearly.

## Integration into final report writing
The structured reporting outputs feed directly into final report writing.

For each case, the final report should now include:

- question-by-question analysis
- a matrix for each question
- richer quotation support from multiple participants
- tables summarising major response patterns
- a theme prominence table
- charts or graphs where useful
- participant summary sheets as background reporting infrastructure

For the final combined report, the report should include:

- cross-case matrices
- cross-case prominence tables
- selected charts or heatmaps comparing cases
- quotation coverage across stakeholder groups

Quality is supported through documentation density, evidential traceability, and analytic transparency.

The methodology uses the following quality supports:

- protocol-based corpus definition
- explicit case boundaries
- file inventories and case registers
- processing logs
- audit-trail entries
- reflexive memos
- coding records and codebooks
- candidate theme development records
- excerpt banks and supporting evidence files
- question-level analytic matrices
- participant-level summary sheets

These materials do not eliminate interpretation. Instead, they make the analytic process open to inspection and help show how conclusions were built from the documented workflow.

## Limitations
This methodology has several inherent limits.

Interpretive thematic analysis does not produce value-free findings, and it does not aim for mechanical replication of results. The quality of interpretation depends on disciplined analytic judgement, transparent documentation, and careful return to source materials.

The corpus may also contain uneven source density across cases, compressed note-based materials, partial pairing ambiguity between source files, and meaning risks associated with mixed-language material. These issues do not prevent analysis, but they require caution and explicit documentation.

The corpus may also contain structured recommendation sheets that compress, group, or clean workshop suggestions. These can strengthen action-oriented reporting, but they also create a risk of over-reading summary rows as if they were direct participant speech. This risk is managed by treating them as auxiliary structured outputs unless row-level traceability shows otherwise.

Cross-case synthesis also introduces a risk of flattening stakeholder-specific meanings. For that reason, the methodology preserves within-case analysis first and treats cross-case comparison as a later, distinct interpretive stage.

## Ethical and governance considerations
The methodology is designed to reduce unnecessary handling risk and preserve responsible use of source materials.

Raw files are preserved in place. The analysis workspace operates as a derivative layer rather than a replacement for the raw corpus. Provenance is maintained so that derived outputs remain linked to their sources without redistributing or altering the original files.

Where source materials contain potentially sensitive qualitative content, interpretation is handled through bounded analytic outputs rather than uncontrolled copying of raw material across the workspace.

## Summary
In practical terms, the full project methodology involves:

1. defining the corpus and case structure
2. separating raw and derived materials
3. documenting provenance and processing decisions
4. familiarising within each stakeholder case
5. preserving discussion context without turning guide questions into themes
6. coding flexible meaning units within cases
7. memoing reflexive and methodological decisions
8. developing and refining within-case themes
9. building question-level matrices, participant summaries, and theme evidence sheets
10. generating descriptive tables and visuals from structured reporting sheets
11. refreshing the corpus and rerunning affected cases when materially expanded core data are added
12. conducting cross-case comparison after case-level theme development
13. producing a final synthesis supported by a transparent audit trail

This methodology provides a standalone, case-sensitive, reflexive thematic framework for analysing the full multi-case dataset.
