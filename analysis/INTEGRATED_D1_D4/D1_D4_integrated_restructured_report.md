The Good Life Across the Life Course

Definition:
- What is Wellbeing? What does it mean to be Well?
  - Overall meaning among all subgroups (life span)
    - Across the four workshops, this merged opening definition material carries substantial analytic weight. The integrated summary tables show that the question on what wellbeing means contributes `227` participant segments and the question on what it means to be well contributes `232`, giving `459` participant segments and `152` summed participant-speaker counts across the opening definition layer. This matters because participants spend a large amount of the discussion defining the standard against which later service experience is judged.
    - Across all four groups, wellbeing is not described as only the absence of illness. Participants describe it as a fuller state that includes balance, meaning, peace, connection, dignity, coping, and the ability to live daily life in a good way. In Arabic, `الحياة الطيبة` carries ideas such as `الرضا`, acceptance, moral steadiness, and balanced living, so spiritual grounding is treated as part of wellbeing rather than as something separate from it.
    - The integrated theme overview strengthens this reading. The most prominent cross-case theme in the full D1-D4 synthesis is wellbeing as a multidimensional, morally grounded balance (`437` participant segments), followed by wellbeing as relationally embedded (`376` participant segments). The integrated question-by-theme matrix shows the same pattern in the opening definition material: multidimensional balance and relational ecology are much stronger than service-fragmentation or redesign themes, which means participants begin by defining the good life before they begin criticizing systems.
    - Participants also make a clear distinction between `being well` and `looking well`. A person may seem calm, productive, medically stable, spiritually composed, or socially functional and still be carrying distress that other people cannot see. This distinction is one of the most important findings in the opening definition material because it warns against taking appearance, performance, or outward order as reliable proof of actual wellbeing.
    - The later life-stage material shows that this hidden-distress pattern changes form across the life course rather than disappearing. In childhood, distress may appear indirectly through behaviour, silence, or withdrawal. In youth, functioning may continue while emotional struggle remains hidden behind peer pressure, shame, or fear of judgment. In adulthood, role competence can become the mask that hides strain. In older age, politeness, restraint, or non-burden behaviour can conceal loneliness, fear, or unmet emotional need. The opening definition layer therefore already contains a life-course warning about superficial assessment.
    - Across the material, wellbeing is also treated as something lived in context. It is shaped by the home, the family, the school, the workplace, the clinic, and the wider social and moral environment. This means being well is both personal and relational. The individual person matters, but the person is repeatedly understood through the environment that carries, weakens, or disturbs that person’s wellbeing.
    - The Arabic and English materials converge on this point from slightly different angles. The Arabic discussion often carries the language of contentment, moral orientation, usefulness to self and others, and balance across life domains. The English discussion often emphasizes coping, purpose, connection, resilience, dignity, and functioning. Read together, these are not two different models of wellbeing. They form one integrated model in which spiritual, moral, relational, emotional, and practical life are treated as inseparable.
    - This opening definition work is methodologically important for the whole report. It means later criticism of healthcare should not be read as vague dissatisfaction or simple preference. Participants first define wellbeing in broad, whole-person, morally and socially grounded terms, and only then assess whether current services help people live that kind of life. The opening definition section therefore establishes the benchmark that the rest of the report keeps returning to.

    - Childhood
      - Across childhood, wellbeing and being well are described not as a narrow clinical state but as a condition of balanced contentment, emotional steadiness, spiritual grounding, and felt safety within a caring environment. Across the two opening childhood discussion blocks, the preserved evidence base contains `157` participant segments, which makes this definition layer conceptually important rather than incidental.
      - The childhood material brings together three linked ideas. First, wellbeing includes contentment and inner steadiness rather than simple happiness. Second, it includes balance across the child’s physical, emotional, spiritual, and social life rather than strength in one domain alone. Third, it includes protection: the child is well when the environment around the child is safe enough for growth, trust, regulation, and ordinary development.
      - This means childhood wellbeing is not judged by symptoms alone. Participants repeatedly interpret the child together with the family, the school, and the service setting around the child. In this material, the child is rarely treated as an isolated unit. Instead, wellbeing is read through relationships, routines, responsiveness, and the quality of the care ecology surrounding the child.
      - The childhood discussion also distinguishes between a broad account of wellbeing and a more specific account of being well. Being well is described as an integrated state across the pillars rather than a single sign of happiness, obedience, or outward calm. That is why surface presentation is treated cautiously: a child may look fine, behave well, or appear settled and still be struggling in ways that adults have failed to notice.
      - This caution matters even more because the childhood opening layer is dominated mainly by professional, educational, and therapeutic voices rather than strong direct child voice. That is not only a limitation of the evidence. It is also part of the finding. Childhood wellbeing is being understood through adult observation and professional interpretation, which means the source base repeatedly assumes that adults must notice, interpret, and respond to what children cannot always name clearly on their own.
      - Childhood material therefore gives wellbeing a strongly relational and preventive meaning. Safety, routine, warmth, meaningful attention, and supportive relationships matter because distress may appear as behaviour, silence, withdrawal, or irritability rather than as a clear verbal request for help. The opening childhood definition is therefore less a description of inner feeling alone than a description of the conditions under which a child can remain emotionally, socially, and developmentally held.

    - Young Adults
      - Across young adulthood, wellbeing and being well are described not as symptom absence or simple happiness, but as a workable balance between health, happiness, purpose, connectedness, coping, emotional regulation, and the capacity to continue ordinary life under pressure. Across the two opening youth discussion blocks, the preserved evidence base contains `71` participant segments, with dominant signals around multidimensional balance, support as a core of wellbeing, contentment, daily functioning, and spiritual or moral grounding.
      - The youth material brings together several linked ideas. First, wellbeing is balanced across multiple domains rather than located in one feeling or one pillar alone. Second, it is morally and relationally grounded through `الرضا`, usefulness to self and others, and the presence of good people around the young person. Third, it is practical: being well means remaining able to function, cope, study, work, and continue daily life without losing inner steadiness.
      - This means youth wellbeing is closely tied to belonging, peer life, identity, and the ability to speak honestly about distress. It is not only a private feeling. It is also social and relational, shaped by whether the young person feels seen, accepted, connected, and safe enough to ask for help.
      - The youth opening layer also adds a stronger functionality component than the childhood material. Several speakers define being well not as perfection, but as ordinary livability: getting up, doing what needs to be done, keeping some purpose, staying emotionally regulated, and still having room for relationships and what the heart wants. This gives the youth material a distinctly practical tone. A young person is not described as well only when life is easy, but when life remains livable and meaningful even under strain.
      - At the same time, this functional emphasis carries an important analytic tension. If wellness is judged partly through productivity, coping, or outward participation, then distress can remain hidden behind apparently normal functioning. The youth opening definition therefore already prepares one of the major concerns that returns later in the case: that silence, shame, or social pressure may conceal real distress behind continued performance.
      - The source base also shows that youth wellbeing is more explicitly socially embedded than a narrow individual model would allow. Participants repeatedly connect wellbeing to relationships, social usefulness, emotional comfort, and being connected to others. A young person may be physically healthy and still not be well if purpose is weak, relationships are strained, or social belonging is absent.
      - Youth material also shows that wellbeing includes moving through study, relationships, and identity formation without becoming cut off from support. In this stage, belonging and honest communication matter because fear of judgment can quickly turn distress into silence, withdrawal, or over-focus on functioning alone.
      - Although professional voices still dominate the clearest preserved evidence, the youth case more directly names the lived textures of this life stage than the childhood case does. Purpose, usefulness, self-respect, social connection, emotional regulation, and daily functioning are all treated as part of what it means to live well as a young person rather than as secondary outcomes.

    - Adults
      - Across adulthood, wellbeing and being well are described not as simple happiness or symptom control, but as an integrated condition of balance, contentment, coping, moral-spiritual grounding, and the ability to keep life workable under pressure. Across the two opening adult discussion blocks, the preserved evidence base contains `159` participant segments, which gives this opening adult definition layer substantial weight in the overall case.
      - The adult material brings together several linked ideas. First, wellbeing is a matter of inner steadiness rather than surface success alone. Second, it is multidimensional, involving spiritual, emotional, social, physical, and practical life at the same time. Third, it is tested through responsibility. Adults speak about wellbeing through work, family, caregiving, judgment, and the effort of continuing to function without losing direction.
      - This means adult wellbeing is often judged through the fit between inner orientation and outer role demands. Being well is not presented as a purely private state sealed off from social life. It is described through the ability to cope with stressors, participate in roles that matter, remain aligned with one’s values, and continue carrying obligations without becoming inwardly fragmented.
      - The adult opening layer also adds a stronger role-bearing and responsibility-centered logic than either childhood or youth. Several speakers describe adulthood as a stage in which wellbeing is negotiated through what a person must carry for self and others: work, family, caregiving, decision-making, and the expectation of continuing to function. This gives adult wellbeing a pressured and ethically charged quality. People are not only asking whether they feel well, but whether they are living in a way that remains bearable, responsible, and coherent.
      - At the same time, the adult material strongly resists reducing wellbeing to outward performance. Participants repeatedly imply that visible competence can conceal real disturbance. An adult may still be working, caregiving, attending appointments, and appearing capable while carrying spiritual strain, emotional imbalance, exhaustion, or private distress. This is why the adult definition layer repeatedly returns to inner balance and coping rather than external performance alone.
      - The bilingual adult evidence also matters analytically. In the English material, wellbeing is often phrased through coping, balanced participation, and maintaining a livable rhythm under personal and work strain. In the Arabic material, the definition carries stronger language of `الرضا`, adaptation, faith, acceptance, and the moral or spiritual ordering of life. Read together, these do not form separate models. They form one adult account in which coping, values, faith, relationships, and daily responsibilities are inseparable.
      - Adult material also shows that wellbeing is deeply relational. Family, close circles, society, and the roles one occupies within them are not peripheral to adult wellbeing; they are part of its structure. A person may appear functional and still not be well if relationships are strained, values and actions are misaligned, or the burden of adult life is being carried without inner steadiness.
      - The adult discussion therefore gives wellbeing a practical, pressured, and morally interpretive quality. Being well is not only feeling calm. It also means holding together stress, roles, values, and responsibilities in a way that remains livable from within.

    - Elderly
      - Across older age, wellbeing and being well are described not as simple medical stability or the absence of visible decline, but as an integrated condition of peace, dignity, acceptance, autonomy, support, and continued personhood. Across the two opening older-age discussion blocks, the preserved evidence base contains `72` participant segments, making this a focused but conceptually rich opening layer in the Day 4 case.
      - The older-age material brings together several linked ideas. First, wellbeing is multidimensional: spiritual, emotional, physical, mental, and social life are treated as inseparable. Second, it is dignity-preserving: an older person is not well if care quietly strips away respect, privacy, voice, or worth. Third, it is relational: wellbeing depends not only on the elder’s body or mood, but on whether the elder is held with containment, recognition, and meaningful support.
      - This means older-age wellbeing is deeply tied to dignity, not feeling like a burden, and careful attention to what may be hidden behind politeness, silence, or outward calm used to protect others or preserve respect. Older people are repeatedly described as capable of minimizing their own difficulties so as not to trouble family members or caregivers. This makes surface presentation a weak guide to real wellbeing.
      - The older-age opening layer therefore resists a narrow medical definition of wellness. A person may be clinically stable, compliant with treatment, and physically quiet and still not be well if companionship, peace, decision-making respect, meaningful autonomy, usefulness, or emotional steadiness are missing from everyday life. Day 4 repeatedly insists that older-age care must look beyond symptoms toward the whole lived setting around the person.
      - The Day 4 material also gives unusual weight to the elder’s wider environment. Several speakers define wellness not only through the individual body, but through the house, the availability of support, the safety of the surroundings, the quality of communication, and whether anyone is really noticing how the person is living. This is why the older-age definition material repeatedly moves toward a `360-degree` view rather than a narrow encounter-based judgment.
      - Another distinctive feature of the older-age material is that it ties wellbeing to continued social significance. Older people are not described simply as recipients of care. They are described as people whose place, contribution, wisdom, privacy, and self-respect still matter. Older-age wellbeing therefore includes remaining recognized as someone of value rather than someone reduced to dependence or decline.
      - The bilingual spread deepens this picture. In the English material, wellbeing is often phrased through peaceful living, satisfaction, integration, and the warning that elders may hide problems to avoid burdening others. In the Arabic material, the definition is more strongly inflected by `الرضا`, `راحة البال`, faith, containment, dignity, and the need to preserve the elder’s place and respect. Read together, these do not form competing models. They form one older-age account in which peace, faith, support, autonomy, and recognition are inseparable.
      - The older-age discussion therefore gives wellbeing a particularly interpretive quality. To say that an elder is well is not merely to describe the state of a body. It is to judge whether a whole person remains held, respected, emotionally steady, socially connected, and able to live with peace and worth despite limitation or dependency.

  - Overall all comparison between all themes (between age groups and between providers and users).
    - Differences and tensions
      - Across all four life stages, wellbeing is never reduced to symptom absence alone. What remains stable is the insistence on multidimensional balance; what changes is the life-stage center of gravity. Childhood centers contentment, safety, and developmental holding. Youth centers balance between health, happiness, purpose, belonging, and livable daily functioning. Adulthood centers coping, inner steadiness, value-orientation, and carrying roles under pressure. Older age centers peace, dignity, non-burden, supportive containment, and continued social worth.
      - The relational location of wellbeing also shifts across age. In childhood, wellbeing is read mainly through the care ecology around the child: family, school, routine, and adult responsiveness. In youth, peers, belonging, and honest communication become more central. In adulthood, wellbeing is negotiated through work, family, caregiving, and social responsibility. In older age, the focus moves toward support networks, respectful care, being heard, and whether the wider living environment protects dignity.
      - Between providers and users, the difference is usually not whether wellbeing is holistic, but how it is recognized. Provider-positioned voices more often describe balance through assessable domains such as functioning, coping, stability, and multidimensional review. User-positioned voices more often stress felt meaning: contentment, usefulness, respect, being listened to, not being a burden, and feeling held within relationships.
      - This split is clearest in the older-age material because the preserved Day 4 evidence contains both clinical and family-positioned definitions. A provider says, `From a doctor’s perspective, "well" means they are medically stable ... not depressed or isolated` (`Elderly · Consultant in family medicine, PHCC, Table 10`), while a user-positioned family voice says, `As a daughter, I say my mother is well because of how she carries herself` (`Elderly · Student, HBKU, Table 10`). The first privileges clinical and psychosocial stability; the second reads wellbeing through dignity, character, presence, and the elder’s maintained sense of self.
      - Childhood requires more caution in provider-versus-user comparison because the opening definition layer is more heavily mediated by professional and adult interpretation than by strong preserved direct child voice. This does not weaken the childhood material. It shows that childhood wellbeing is often known indirectly, through whether adults can recognize what the child cannot yet fully articulate.
      - The hidden-distress pattern changes by life stage but remains analytically central throughout. Children may look settled while struggling; young adults may keep functioning while carrying shame, disconnection, or pressure; adults may hide distress behind competence and responsibility; older people may minimize needs out of politeness or to avoid burdening others. Across the life course, visible normality is therefore a weak proxy for wellbeing.
      - Another cross-case tension lies between inward experience and outward performance. Youth and adult material especially link being well to functioning, coping, and participation, but both cases also warn that functioning can conceal suffering. Older-age material extends this warning by showing that even calmness, compliance, or medical stability may coexist with loneliness, loss of dignity, or unheard needs.
      - The bilingual evidence sharpens the comparison further. Across youth, adulthood, and older age, English material often emphasizes balance, coping, functioning, and participation, while Arabic material more strongly foregrounds `الرضا`, faith, `راحة البال`, acceptance, and moral-spiritual ordering. These are not rival definitions. Together they show that wellbeing across the life course is both practical and existential.
      - Cross-age comparison should therefore be interpretive rather than hierarchical. Childhood and adulthood have the largest opening-definition evidence bases (`157` and `159` participant segments), while youth and older age are smaller but still conceptually rich (`71` and `72`). The comparison is strongest when treated as a shift in emphasis across the life course rather than a claim that one age group cares about wellbeing more than another.

  - Short summary:
    - Why is it important
      - These opening questions set the standard for the whole report because they define what later sections must try to protect, improve, or restore. If wellbeing is understood this broadly, later judgments about services cannot be reduced to symptoms, speed, or physical treatment alone.
      - The opening definition material shows that wellbeing is not a single outcome. It is a multidimensional condition involving emotional steadiness, social connection, spiritual or moral orientation, physical livability, dignity, and the practical ability to live daily life in a way that still feels bearable and meaningful.
      - This matters because the life-course evidence also shows that the meaning of being well changes with age. A child needs safety, holding, and developmental support. A young person needs belonging, purpose, and room to speak honestly. An adult needs livable balance under role pressure. An older person needs dignity, peaceful support, and protection from being reduced to burden or decline. A single narrow assessment model cannot capture all of these equally well.
      - It also matters because the material repeatedly warns against relying on outward presentation alone. Across all four age groups, people may look functional, calm, settled, or compliant while still carrying distress that is hidden by silence, shame, competence, politeness, or dependence on others. The report therefore needs this opening definition layer in order to interpret all later findings with the right level of caution.
      - Finally, this section matters because it shows that provider and user voices are not interchangeable. Clinical and professional views often clarify structure, domains, and observable indicators, while user and family-positioned voices more clearly reveal meaning, dignity, burden, belonging, and whether life still feels worth living from within. Both are necessary if wellbeing is to be assessed seriously.
    - Policy indications and recommendations
      - Assessment standards should move beyond biomedical stability and include psychological, social, spiritual, relational, and everyday-life wellbeing, so that services are evaluated against the fuller definition participants themselves describe.
      - Multi-domain assessment should be strengthened at major transition points such as school entry, adolescence, early adulthood, major family or work-role change, and retirement, because the evidence shows that pressures and meanings shift across the life course rather than staying fixed.
      - Assessment and review tools should be more age-attuned. Childhood review should pay attention to safety, regulation, family ecology, and adult responsiveness. Youth review should include belonging, purpose, support, and hidden distress behind functioning. Adult review should include role strain, coping burden, and the gap between performance and inner steadiness. Older-age review should include dignity, autonomy, social meaning, and the lived environment around the elder.
      - Hidden distress should be treated as a cross-life-stage issue, and staff should be trained to recognize age-specific concealment patterns rather than assuming that outward calm, school participation, productivity, treatment compliance, or politeness prove that a person is well.
      - Elderly review should include a `360-degree` assessment of social environment, dignity, communication, burden, and psychological state rather than physical function alone, because Day 4 material shows that older-age wellbeing is especially vulnerable to being misread when viewed too narrowly.
      - Service evaluation should separate provider experience, service-user experience, and family or caregiver experience where relevant, because these positions illuminate different parts of the same problem and should not be collapsed into one account.
      - Measures of success should include whether people feel heard, respected, connected, and able to live meaningfully, not only whether symptoms are reduced or appointments are delivered quickly.
      - Policy design should therefore treat wellbeing as a whole-person and whole-context outcome, which means that improvement may require work across several pillars at once rather than isolated intervention in only one domain.

Framework:
- How do the five pillars contribute to improving wellbeing?
  - Overall meaning among all subgroups (life span)
    - This is one of the richest parts of the evidence. Across the integrated summary tables, the five-pillar question is the most conceptually developed discussion in the whole dataset, with `Q3` containing `570` participant segments. Participants use it not only to name the pillars, but to explain how wellbeing is built, sustained, weakened, and restored across ordinary life.
    - Participants do not describe the five pillars as a simple checklist or as five separate service categories. They describe them as connected parts of one whole. In this material, weakness in one pillar can disturb the others, and improvement in one domain is rarely enough on its own if the surrounding pillars remain unstable.
    - The framework is also value-laden rather than purely technical. The pillars are repeatedly linked to meaning, responsibility, care, reflection, truthfulness, self-regulation, prevention, and spiritual cultivation. This gives the framework moral and cultural depth rather than treating wellbeing as a neutral act of self-management.
    - Two patterns appear again and again. The spiritual pillar is often described as the source of meaning, grounding, patience, and resilience, while the social pillar is the main route through which the other pillars are carried in daily life through family, peers, caregivers, institutions, and community.
    - The remaining pillars are not secondary. The emotional pillar helps explain steadiness, distress, regulation, and the ability to remain inwardly balanced. The physical pillar anchors health, strength, energy, and daily livability. The intellectual pillar appears through awareness, judgment, learning, understanding, and the ability to make sense of oneself and one’s circumstances.
    - Across the life course, the same framework keeps its core meaning but changes in emphasis. In childhood it is carried mainly through adults and institutions. In youth it becomes tightly linked to belonging, identity, and emotional safety. In adulthood it is tested by role pressure, coping, and practical balance. In older age it becomes closely tied to dignity, peace, support, and the wider environment around the person.
    - The framework therefore works as more than a list of values. It is a way of seeing how the pillars shape one another, why a person can look stable in one pillar while still struggling overall, and why real improvement often depends on several pillars being strengthened together rather than on isolated intervention in one area alone.

  - Short summary:
    - Why is it important
      - This section matters because it shows that the five-pillar framework has practical meaning for participants across the full life course. It is not only a concept or a values list. It is one of the main ways people explain how wellbeing is built, sustained, weakened, and restored in ordinary life, which fits with the integrated evidence weight of `Q3` at `570` participant segments.
      - It is also important because the section shows that the same framework does different work at different life stages. In childhood it is carried through adults and institutions, in youth through belonging and emotional safety, in adulthood through negotiation between self-responsibility and system responsibility, and in older age through dignity, non-burden, usefulness, and spiritual steadiness. This means the framework is stable in principle but variable in application.
    - Policy indications and recommendations
      - Implementation should be life-stage responsive rather than identical for all age groups, because the same pillars are carried through different social ecologies at different points in the life course.
      - Training and service design should treat the pillars as connected and socially carried, not as separate checklist items, since the evidence repeatedly shows that weakness in one domain can destabilize the others.
      - Schools, families, clinics, home-care services, rehabilitation settings, and community spaces should all be treated as delivery sites for the pillars, not as optional extras outside “real” healthcare.
      - Provider-reported framework knowledge and user-reported pillar experience should be measured separately, and mixed-role voices should be interpreted carefully, so service claims are not confused with lived experience.
      - Institutional policy should apply dual accountability so wellbeing is not framed as individual responsibility alone, especially where adults, families, or older people are being asked to absorb structural strain.
      - Quality improvement should include explanation, health literacy, communication, dignity, privacy, and culturally grounded support as core indicators of pillar-based care, not only physical throughput or service availability.

- Are the HT pillars reflected in the healthcare services currently provided?
  - Overall meaning among all subgroups (life span)
    - Across the four groups, services reflect the pillars only partly and unevenly. Participants do not usually say that whole-person care is entirely absent. Rather, they describe a system in which the pillars appear intermittently, unevenly, and with much stronger support for the physical domain than for the emotional, social, spiritual, and intellectual ones.
    - The integrated pattern is not only about weak delivery but about a mismatch between endorsed values and routine practice. In the cross-case theme matrix, `Q4` is the point where `IT3` becomes most visible at `110` participant segments, while `IT1` (`74`), `IT2` (`71`), and `IT4` (`69`) remain strongly present. This shows that participants are still judging services through a multidimensional and relational understanding of wellbeing, but they repeatedly find that actual care remains pressured, fragmented, and only partly aligned with that wider standard.
    - The spiritual pillar shows this clearly, but the pattern is broader than spirituality alone. Prayer space, respectful religious language, or occasional holistic gestures may be present, yet spiritually sensitive, emotionally attentive, socially aware, and explanatory care still depends too much on individual staff, available time, or a particular setting rather than on a stable institutional model. Across the life course, participants keep distinguishing between isolated examples of good care and a reliable system that can be expected to hold the five pillars together.
    - The main issue is therefore not total absence of the pillars but weak consistency and weak embedding. Some patients and families do encounter services that reflect whole-person care, but others meet rushed, narrow, or problem-specific care that falls back to the physical issue only. This makes `Q4` the clearest turning point from ideal to service reality: the framework is broadly accepted in principle, yet its reflection in current healthcare remains partial, provider-dependent, and unevenly institutionalized.

    - Policy indications and recommendations
      - Move from provider-dependent examples of good practice to explicit whole-person service standards, so integrated care does not depend mainly on the commitment of individual clinicians, teams, or pilot sites.
      - Reduce over-concentration on the physical pillar by strengthening communication, continuity, explanation, question safety, and relational trust, especially in services where rushed encounters make emotional and social needs harder to surface.
      - Audit services against all five pillars rather than judging quality only through speed, throughput, waiting-time management, and biomedical outcomes.
      - Treat spiritual care as a clinical competency rather than assuming infrastructure alone is enough, and ensure it is linked to dignity, communication, and culturally respectful whole-person support.
      - Design implementation by life stage rather than as a single uniform model: childhood needs coordination around the child and family, youth needs disclosure safety and stigma-sensitive access, adulthood needs explanation and navigation support, and older age needs continuity, dignity, and reliable integrated follow-through.

  - Short summary:
    - Why is it important
      - This section shows that the main barriers are structural and relational rather than conceptual. With `Q5` carrying `387` participant segments and `67` summed participant-speaker counts, participants already understand the value of whole-person care more clearly than the system currently delivers it.

    - Policy indications and recommendations
      - Reform should focus on time, continuity, explanation, trust, and coordination rather than awareness alone, because participants repeatedly describe the main failures as operational and relational rather than as lack of conceptual agreement.
      - Rights literacy for patients and communication support for providers should be treated as core quality issues, especially in adult care where hierarchy, explanation, and navigational confidence shape whether whole-person care can actually be used.
      - Confidentiality and disclosure safety should be treated as quality-of-care issues, especially for young people, because disclosure failure blocks access to the wider pillars before care can become genuinely holistic.
      - Childhood and older-age redesign should prioritize participation and dignity in practical terms: family-inclusive decision making, coordinated listening around the child, and elder-sensitive communication that makes inclusion, respect, and the social pillar concrete rather than rhetorical.

Policymaking sections:

  - What practical suggestions were raised for improving healthcare services through the pillars?
  - Across the four workshops, participants did not ask for small adjustments. With `Q7` carrying `163` participant segments and `34` summed participant-speaker counts, they describe practical integration as redesign rather than goodwill alone. They want healthcare to move from narrow, rushed, visit-by-visit care to person-centred, rights-based, community-linked care with better time, clearer pathways, stronger coordination, and more explicit accountability.
  - The practical tone here matters because the integrated `Q7` pattern is dominated by `IT4 = 43`, with much lighter but still relevant presence for `IT3 = 6`, `IT2 = 3`, and `IT1 = 2`. Participants are therefore not mainly using this question to restate the philosophy of HT. They are using it to specify what institutions, pathways, standards, workforce capacity, and governance arrangements would need to change for the pillars to shape care in everyday practice.
  - In childhood, the strongest practical suggestions focus on starting early through schools, family support, workforce training, community awareness, and better coordination across child services. This is also the thinnest direct `Q7` case methodologically: the clearest surviving material is structured practical-suggestion content rather than a dense quotation layer. Even so, the direction is consistent. Participants want earlier notice, stronger staff preparation, and child support that is built across school, family, and service settings rather than left to crisis response.
  - In young adulthood, the practical suggestions focus on safer communication, awareness, and help-seeking routes that feel private and non-judgmental. One youth speaker recommended awareness that explains `what are the signs` and reassures young people that they `will get help without being judged` (`Youth · Specialist family medicine, PHCC, Table 9`). The emphasis is on support that feels private enough to use, clear enough to trust, and socially safe enough not to shame the person who seeks it.
  - In adulthood, the practical suggestions become more structural and more explicitly institutional. Participants ask for one-visit integrated care, stronger staff preparation, and formal training and relicensing requirements. This is stated directly in the adult recommendation to `دمج الركائز الخمسة للحياة الطيبة بشكل متكامل في الخدمات الصحية ... وتجديد التراخيص المهنية` (`Adults · participant contribution with incomplete speaker attribution, Table 6`). This is structural rather than cosmetic change, though the adult `Q7` layer still needs attribution caution because some of the clearest redesign material survives as later contributions with incomplete speaker attribution.
  - In older age, the practical suggestions are the strongest and most extensive in `Q7`. Elderly material moves beyond general endorsement toward dignity-focused redesign, communication training, professional development, elder-specific social-health supports, and expansion of models such as `I-COPE`. The call for support that can be found `بمكان واحد` and for HT-linked training to count within professional development (`Elderly · Occupational therapy, elderly care, Hamad Medical Corporation, Table 3`; `Elderly · Service recipient / patient voice, Table 9`) shows that participants are asking not only for more services, but for integrated, community-based support that is easier to find, easier to use, and less dependent on individual goodwill.
  - Across age groups, the practical pathway becomes more institutionally demanding over time: early promotion and prevention through family-school support in childhood, trust-building, stigma reduction, and youth-safe communication in youth, pathway and workforce redesign in adulthood, and dignity-sensitive institutional embedding in older age.
  - Between providers and users, the difference is also clear, but it is a difference in emphasis more than direction. Provider-positioned voices more often describe training, standards, pathways, licensing, routine expectations, and system design. User-positioned or lived-positioned voices, especially in older age, more often describe what good redesign should feel like in practice: accessible, respectful, joined up, and possible to use in daily life without loss of dignity.
  - A cross-case caution is also necessary here. The practical redesign logic is strong, but the detail is not equally preserved across all life stages. Childhood has the thinnest direct suggestion layer, adulthood has stronger redesign logic than speaker attribution in some rows, and older age carries the richest practical detail. The common direction is still stable: participants want durable institutional change rather than isolated goodwill.
  - Taken together, these recommendations align with the broader policy direction visible across the referenced national and summit documents: integrated quality services, stronger community-based and primary-care routes, promotion and prevention across the life course, stigma reduction, investment in workforce capacity, meaningful involvement of people with lived experience, enabling governance frameworks, and measurable implementation rather than endorsement alone. In the workshop evidence itself, participants want the pillars to shape pathways, standards, training, communication, and community linkage, not just the language used around care.
  - What I need from decision makers in health sectors to reach HT for each person in Qatar?
    - Across the life course, participants want decision makers to do more than approve the idea of HT. With `Q7` carrying `163` participant segments and `34` summed participant-speaker counts, and with `IT4` dominant at `43` segments, the leadership ask is chiefly institutional: participants want the pillars embedded in governance, service design, workforce expectations, and implementation routines rather than left at the level of values talk.
    - In other words, participants want decision makers to convert HT from a valued concept into an operating standard and measurable implementation agenda. They are asking for leadership choices that shape service time, access rules, workforce capacity, training, licensing, community-based and primary-care-linked pathways, and how success is reviewed in practice.
    - In childhood, decision makers are asked to make the framework organizational, train staff, and make services available across settings. This is stated clearly in the call for services and training to be `متوفرة بكل المراكز` (`Childhood · Healthcare professional, Table 2`). Because the direct childhood `Q7` layer is thinner than later life stages, the leadership request should be read as one of reach and infrastructure: build promotion, prevention, and early support into schools, families, and child-facing services rather than leaving childhood HT to isolated programmes or motivated individuals.
    - In youth, the main leadership need is trusted access in settings that feel safe enough to use. Young people need school and health routes where they can speak safely and `get help without being judged` (`Youth · Specialist family medicine, PHCC, Table 9`). The request is not only for access in principle, but for privacy, non-judgment, public awareness, and stigma reduction to be designed into the route itself so help-seeking is possible before crisis.
    - In adulthood, participants ask decision makers to formalize integrated one-visit care, staff formation, and licensing expectations instead of leaving them to personal enthusiasm. They are asking for adult care quality to stop depending on whether one clinician happens to take a broader view and for workforce development to become part of implementation rather than an afterthought. Here the governance ask is explicit: redesign pathways, standards, and professional preparation so whole-person care is routine rather than optional.
    - In older age, decision makers are asked to make HT part of institutional culture, formal planning, and accountable service organization. An elderly public-sector leader said this approach must become `جزء من الخطة العامة للمؤسسة الصحية` (`Elderly · Senior public-sector leader, Table 7`). Combined with calls for one-place access, communication training, professional development, and elder-specific social-health supports, the request is for dignity to be planned for, monitored, and supported rather than improvised, and for elder care to be embedded within community-linked and institutionally accountable pathways.
    - Across age groups, the leadership need shifts from service reach and readiness in childhood, to youth-safe trust systems, to adult pathway and licensing reform, and then to elder-care cultural and institutional embedding. This mirrors the broader `Q7` redesign logic: practical integration becomes more real when institutions deliberately connect communication, standards, training, and access around different life-stage needs.
    - This part of the evidence is more provider-heavy because it is about governance. Even so, user-positioned elderly evidence sharpens the point by stressing one-place access, usable services, dignity, and staff behaviour in daily practice. The provider/user difference is therefore not about different goals, but about different vantage points on what accountable implementation must deliver.
    - This makes the governance question central. Without institutional backing, HT stays dependent on motivated individuals rather than becoming a normal expectation across the system. The policy-facing implication is not only leadership endorsement, but person-centred, rights-based, measurable implementation through accountable pathways, workforce capacity, routine review, and stronger community-based and primary-care-linked routes where appropriate.
  - How HT pillar encourages community engagement (including health providers, and other sectors)?
    - The HT model encourages community engagement because the workshops consistently describe wellbeing as something carried through relationships, institutions, and everyday settings, not produced by the clinic alone. In the integrated `Q7` profile, where `IT4 = 43` is far stronger than `IT3 = 6`, `IT2 = 3`, and `IT1 = 2`, participants are not mainly asking for more compassionate language. They are describing how the pillars should travel through schools, families, universities, media, home-care routes, and other community-linked systems.
    - This question therefore shows that community engagement is not a side issue. It is one of the main ways the pillars become operational through multisectoral, community-based, and primary-care-linked action. Across the evidence, engagement means building trusted public routes, strengthening awareness and early help-seeking, and connecting health providers with the institutions where people already live, study, work, age, and seek support.
    - In childhood, this means schools, families, and early awareness routes. A childhood participant said the long-term answer is `implementing it in the teaching system from the get go elementary up` (`Childhood · healthcare professional, Table 0`). The same contribution also asked for `more psychology minded people` to help spread the word. This matters because child wellbeing is carried in daily settings long before it appears in specialist care, so community engagement in childhood is fundamentally about promotion, prevention, and school-family-service linkage.
    - In youth, community engagement means public awareness, school support, private help-seeking, and trusted routes outside highly stigmatized spaces. Youth material recommends a social media campaign so young people can understand `what are the signs`, trust school and healthcare services, and `reach out to counselors privately` without shame (`Youth · Specialist family medicine, PHCC, Table 9`; `Youth · note-taker summary`). The emphasis is on meeting young people where they already communicate and building stigma-reducing, non-judgmental routes that feel safe enough to use.
    - In adulthood, community engagement means universities, campaigns, workplaces, and accessible counselling points that already exist. Adult evidence points to Qatar University as a place that can `provide psycho therapy counseling for free` and also calls for `proper strong campaigns like breast cancer campaigns` (`Adults · Psychologist, Table 4`; `Adults · Head of central laboratories, Hamad Hospital, Table 4`). This links wellbeing to the institutions through which adult life is actually lived and shows that community engagement here is not separate from service design: it works by making support visible in ordinary educational, employment, and public-awareness settings.
    - In older age, community engagement means bringing support closer to daily life through home care, awareness work, family continuity, and community-facing elder support. One elderly participant identified `bringing services directly to the patient through home care` and `preparing the public through awareness campaigns` as key opportunities (`Elderly · Rumailah Hospital participant, Table 10`). A home-care physician further described outreach through social media and television, while insisting that older people should not be unnecessarily separated from the family environment (`Elderly · Home care physician, Table 7`). This means community engagement in older age is closely tied to dignity, reduced isolation, and support that reaches people where they already live and relate.
    - Across age groups, the route shifts from school and parent structures in childhood, to youth-safe communication spaces, to adult institutions and campaigns, and then to home- and family-linked elder support. The life-stage pattern is therefore not random. It follows where people are most socially located and where engagement is most likely to become usable.
    - Provider voices describe the available community routes more clearly because many of these routes are named by professionals who can see service interfaces across sectors. User and family voices sharpen the question by showing which routes feel private, respectful, dignified, and realistic to use. The provider/user difference therefore again concerns emphasis rather than direction: providers describe linkages and delivery points, while lived-positioned voices show the conditions under which those links become trustworthy.
    - The wider message is that HT encourages shared responsibility. Providers, schools, families, universities, employers, community services, media, and other sectors all become part of the wellbeing pathway. This is also why community engagement in this material sits close to promotion, prevention, public dialogue, stigma reduction, and community-based support rather than to clinical care alone.

Moving Forward:
- Creative Solutions through the lens of the five values (`ilm`, `insan`, `turab`, `waqt`, `jamal`)?
  - This part is an interpretive reading of the workshop evidence through the five values. Participants did not use these five labels directly, but the themes fit them closely enough to provide a useful forward-looking lens.
  - `ilm` points to mental health literacy, parent education, service-rights literacy, staff training, research, and clearer explanation inside services.
  - `insan` points to person-centred care, dignity, listening, trust, lived-experience participation, and not reducing people to symptoms or burden.
  - `turab` points to community-based, culturally grounded implementation that fits homes, schools, families, neighbourhoods, mosques, and other local institutions.
  - `waqt` points to time: prevention, earlier support, better continuity, protected listening space, and realistic timelines for implementation.
  - `jamal` points to the felt quality of care: respectful interaction, humane spaces, calm communication, and digital tools that widen access without replacing human relationships.
  - Read together, these five values offer a simple forward lens for design. They translate the report into practical questions: Are people informed, treated with dignity, supported in real community settings, given enough time, and cared for in ways that feel humane?
  - Used this way, the five values do not replace the workshop evidence. They organize it into a forward-looking design language that is easier to apply across policy, service design, communication, and evaluation.
- How HT pillars can cover the gaps currently seen in health care services?
  - The five pillars can help cover current service gaps because they force the system to look beyond symptoms and beyond the short visit. They bring together physical health, emotions, social context, spirituality, and understanding.
  - In childhood, the pillars could close the gap between separated services by linking family, school, and health support earlier and more consistently. This matters where children are pushed through narrow visit rules instead of being seen holistically, and where the child can otherwise be split across systems that each see only part of the picture.
  - In youth, the pillars could close trust and disclosure gaps by making emotional safety, social belonging, and anti-stigma communication part of care rather than an afterthought. This is why confidentiality and trusted access matter so much in the youth material, and why the first contact with care needs to feel usable rather than risky.
  - In adulthood, the pillars could close the gap between treatment and daily life. Adults repeatedly say that getting physically better is not enough if the system does not help with explanation, navigation, follow-up, and living well after the visit. In practice, this means connecting medical care to understanding, routine, and re-entry into ordinary responsibilities.
  - In older age, the pillars could close dignity and continuity gaps by making support more relational, more home-linked, and less dependent on whether an individual doctor has extra time. This would help make whole-person attention a normal part of care rather than an occasional extra.
  - Across the life course, the framework addresses different gaps: coordination in childhood, trust in youth, navigation and explanation in adulthood, and dignity and continuity in older age.
  - The provider-user contrast is important here. Providers usually describe the gaps as staffing, time, standards, and coordination problems. User-positioned voices show how those same gaps are felt as confusion, delay, silence, burden, or loss of dignity.
  - This is why HT is useful going forward. It does not only describe a good life in theory. It gives a practical map for changing assessment, communication, referral, follow-up, and community linkage.
  - Put simply, the pillars are useful because they help the system ask better questions: not only what illness is present, but what support is missing, which relationship is weak, what meaning has been disturbed, and what part of daily life is no longer working well.
- How Al Hayat Al Tayba promote national wellbeing responsibility.
  - Al Hayat Al Tayba promotes national wellbeing responsibility by making it clear that wellbeing is shared. It is not the job of the patient alone, and it is not the job of the health sector alone.
  - In childhood, responsibility sits across family, school, and services around the child, because no single setting sees the whole child on its own.
  - In youth, responsibility extends across peers, families, schools, and trusted health routes, especially where help-seeking depends on trust, privacy, and belonging.
  - In adulthood, responsibility includes institutions that shape work, care access, communication, and rights, because adult wellbeing is shaped as much by system conditions as by personal coping.
  - In older age, responsibility includes family continuity, dignified services, community inclusion, and respectful public language around ageing, because older-age wellbeing depends on being supported without being diminished or socially erased.
  - Across the life course, the social carriers change, but the pattern stays the same: wellbeing needs support from health, education, social care, labour, and community systems together.
  - Provider voices state this system responsibility directly. Adult material says, `we should think individual, but we should think systems` (`Adults · participant contribution with incomplete speaker attribution, Table 3`). User and family voices show why this matters in daily life, especially when older people need care that preserves dignity and does not make them feel like a burden.
  - The national implication is clear: if Qatar wants HT to be real, it needs whole-of-government and whole-of-society implementation, not isolated service reform inside one sector.
  - That also means national responsibility should not turn into blame on individuals or families. The framework points toward shared conditions that make wellbeing more possible or less possible across the life course.
