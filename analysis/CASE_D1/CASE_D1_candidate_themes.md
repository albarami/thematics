# CASE_D1 Candidate Theme Development

## Development process
Candidate themes were developed through iterative engagement with the Day 1 data:
1. Deep familiarisation with all 7 transcripts and 8 note files
2. Systematic coding at meaning-unit level (1158 segments, 811 participant)
3. Code clustering by semantic affinity and analytical function
4. Testing theme boundaries against evidence distribution across questions and tables

## Candidate themes tested

### Candidate A — Contentment, balance, and spiritual-moral grounding as the foundation of wellbeing
- **Status**: RETAINED as Theme 1
- **Core idea**: Participants construct wellbeing not as the absence of problems but as an active spiritual-moral stance: contentment (قناعة), multidimensional balance (توازن), and faith-based acceptance (رضا). This triad functions as the moral lens through which all subsequent discussion of children's reality, challenges, and solutions is framed.
- **Supporting codes**: contentment_as_core (4), balance_multidimensional (13), spiritual_moral_anchor (24), safety_security (2), inner_peace (implicit in familiarisation), pillar_physical (17), pillar_emotional (11), pillar_social (20), pillar_intellectual (10)
- **Evidence spread**: Q1-Q3 primary; echoes in Q4-Q5. Tables 0, 10, with support from 2, 6, 7 (via notes and familiarisation).
- **Analytical sharpness**: This is not a generic "wellbeing is important" theme. It captures a specific cultural-spiritual epistemology of the good life that grounds the entire Day 1 discussion.

### Candidate B — The interdependent professional care ecology around children
- **Status**: RETAINED as Theme 2
- **Core idea**: Participants describe children as embedded within an interconnected system of professionals — clinicians, psychologists, social workers, educators, volunteers, family consultants — who are meant to coordinate around the child. The ecology is described as both aspiration (how it should work) and partial reality (some elements function, others don't).
- **Supporting codes**: interdependent_care (13), school_clinical_link (43), family_support_ecology (27), community_volunteer_bridge (1), professional_identity (13), service_recipient_voice (15)
- **Evidence spread**: Q3-Q5 primary. Tables 0 (strongest), 10, 6.
- **Analytical sharpness**: Captures the structural architecture of care around children as participants see it — not just individual services but their intended interconnection.

### Candidate C — Fragmented, strained, and uneven childhood service conditions
- **Status**: RETAINED as Theme 3
- **Core idea**: The care ecology described in Theme 2 is simultaneously described as broken — services are siloed, training is inadequate, resources are strained, the medical model dominates, community step-down is missing, and bureaucratic barriers prevent improvement. Individual professionals possess adequate knowledge but operate within structures that prevent holistic, connected care.
- **Supporting codes**: system_disconnection (7), training_deficit (10), resource_strain (2), healthcare_system_critique (30), consultation_time_pressure (2), staffing_shortage_isolation (2), medical_model_dominance (2), missing_stepdown_services (1), child_vulnerability (10), child_mental_health (1), bullying_violence (1), child_rights_inclusion (2)
- **Evidence spread**: Q4-Q5 primary; Q3 secondary. Tables 0, 10, 2, 6.
- **Analytical sharpness**: This is not "challenges in healthcare" generically. It captures a specific pattern: structural fragmentation undermining individual professional competence, with children bearing the consequences of systemic disconnection.

### Candidate D — Culturally grounded coordination, early awareness, and formal implementation as routes to change
- **Status**: RETAINED as Theme 4
- **Core idea**: Participants propose solutions that are (a) culturally situated in the Qatari/Gulf context, (b) focused on early detection and community awareness rather than just clinical intervention, and (c) demanding formal institutional/policy implementation rather than relying on individual initiative.
- **Supporting codes**: cultural_local_adaptation (21), transcultural_workforce (2), awareness_education (25), early_detection_prevention (6), practical_recommendation (37), top_down_systemic_change (9), healthcare_worker_wellbeing (3), gradual_implementation (1), digital_platform_proposal (1), respite_inclusive_resources (2)
- **Evidence spread**: Q1-Q5 primary coded support. Q6-Q7 contribute close-reading support only and are not claimed as coded Theme 4 evidence. Tables 0, 10 (strongest), 6.
- **Analytical sharpness**: Solutions are not abstract. They reflect participants' understanding of the specific Qatari context — the expatriate workforce, the public-private divide, the Islamic cultural framework, the need for top-down mandates.

### Candidate E — Hidden distress and the unreliability of appearances
- **Status**: ABSORBED as cross-cutting pattern within Themes 1 and 3
- **Core idea**: Children (and adults) may appear well on the surface while suffering underneath; contentment as an ideal can coexist with hidden distress; services that rely on visible symptoms miss what isn't expressed.
- **Supporting codes**: hidden_masked_distress (2 in automated coding, but substantive presence in familiarisation reading)
- **Evidence spread**: Limited in coded data. Present in HWCH6AR (D1_P10's clinical discussion) and HWCH10AR (wellbeing definitions).
- **Reason for absorption**: Coded evidence is too thin to sustain a standalone theme. The pattern functions analytically within Theme 1 (the spiritual contentment framework can coexist with hidden suffering — a tension) and Theme 3 (fragmented services miss invisible distress — a consequence). It is documented as an important cross-cutting analytical thread rather than a final theme.

### Candidate F — Professional voice dominance (methodological pattern)
- **Status**: NOT a theme — documented as methodological limitation
- **Core idea**: Nearly all identifiable speakers are healthcare/social work professionals. Children's own voices and family/caregiver perspectives are largely absent.
- **Reason for exclusion**: This is a data limitation, not a participant-generated pattern. It is documented in the boundary memo and acknowledged in the final report, but it does not constitute a theme.

## Final theme count: 4 themes + 1 cross-cutting pattern
