## Theme 1: Adult wellbeing as integrated balance, moral-spiritual grounding, and workable coping

### Definition
Day 3 participants define adult wellbeing as an integrated condition rather than a single psychological or biomedical state. Across Arabic and English sources, adults describe being well through balance across the pillars, contentment and acceptance, moral-spiritual grounding, value-action alignment, relationship to others, and the practical ability to cope with adult pressures without collapsing under them. The distinct Day 3 contribution is that these are not treated as separate add-ons: participants repeatedly speak as if adult wellbeing depends on holding them together under real life strain.

### Key questions
- **Primary**: Q1-Q3
- **Secondary**: Q4-Q5, where the same ideal becomes the standard against which participants judge actual healthcare practice

### Key participants and sources
- `D3_P20`, `D3_P21`, `D3_P22`, `D3_P25` (`HWAD10AR`) — wholeness, balance, coping, and adult life-stage burden
- `D3_P01`, `D3_P02` (`HWAD1AR`) — strong Arabic moral-spiritual and acceptance-based framing
- `D3_P15`, `D3_P16` (`HWAD4AR`) — balance, رضا, and explicit spiritual grounding in adult wellbeing
- `D3_P05` plus `D3_U01` (`HWAD3AR`) — personal balance, value alignment, and attribution-cautious support for alignment language

### Tensions within this theme
- Moral-spiritual grounding is central in some Arabic material, but the adult case also includes plural, multicultural, and secular formulations of meaning and balance
- Functioning and coping are treated as parts of wellbeing, yet the case repeatedly suggests that visible functioning can conceal deeper strain
- Participants affirm individual balance, but later themes show how difficult it is to sustain under time-poor and fragmented care conditions

### Supporting codes
`balance_multidimensional`, `contentment_acceptance`, `inner_peace_stability`, `functionality_coping_capacity`, `spiritual_moral_anchor`, `values_action_alignment`, `relational_connectedness`

## Theme 2: Adult wellbeing as negotiated through family, work, caregiving, and role-bearing strain

### Definition
Day 3 treats adulthood as a life stage structured by role load rather than by abstract maturity alone. Participants describe adult wellbeing through family obligation, caregiving, work pressure, parenting, social responsibility, and the burden of managing oneself while still being needed by others. The theme becomes sharper when speakers move between self-responsibility and systemic responsibility: adults are expected to regulate, cope, and act responsibly, but many participants explicitly resist the idea that adult wellbeing can be secured by self-management alone while family, work, or institutional conditions remain misaligned.

### Key questions
- **Primary**: Q1-Q5
- **Secondary**: later questions where the same burden reappears in proposals about access, provider support, and redesign

### Key participants and sources
- `D3_P19`, `D3_P20`, `D3_P21`, `D3_P22`, `D3_P24`, `D3_P25` (`HWAD10AR`) — adult life described through work, parenting, responsibility, and provider/patient overlap
- `D3_P02` (`HWAD1AR`) — family, violence, transport, time, and service access pressures in a protection setting
- `D3_P16` (`HWAD4AR`) — adulthood framed through `ديني ودنياي وأهلي ومالي` rather than a single isolated self
- `D3_P04`, `D3_P05`, `D3_P07` plus `D3_U01` (`HWAD3AR`) — well-adjustment, family burden, and explicit individual-versus-system reasoning with attribution caveat

### Tensions within this theme
- Participants repeatedly endorse self-responsibility, but also insist that institutions should not offload structural failure onto individuals
- Family appears both as support and as a site of pressure, secrecy, stigma, or unequal burden
- Providers often speak as knowledgeable professionals, yet they also describe themselves as adults under strain, not as detached experts outside the problem

### Supporting codes
`adult_role_responsibility`, `family_caregiving_ecology`, `work_life_strain`, `self_responsibility_orientation`, `system_responsibility_orientation`, `provider_self_as_adult`

## Theme 3: Holistic adult care undermined by time pressure, hierarchy, navigation gaps, and uneven communication

### Definition
Participants describe a recurring mismatch between the holistic language of wellbeing and the conditions under which adult care is delivered. In practice, adults and families face limited appointment time, waiting and access problems, unequal health literacy, confusion about referral routes, authority-sensitive interactions, defensive responses to questioning, fragmented coordination, and persistent language-cultural mismatch. The theme is not just that services are imperfect; it is that the very conditions needed for adults to participate meaningfully in care — time, explanation, navigation, trust, and culturally legible communication — are structurally fragile.

### Key questions
- **Primary**: Q3-Q5
- **Secondary**: Q6-Q7, where the same problems shape opportunities and recommendations

### Key participants and sources
- `D3_P20`, `D3_P21`, `D3_P22`, `D3_P24`, `D3_P25` (`HWAD10AR`) — appointment access, provider hierarchy, health-literacy inequality, and fragmented routes
- `D3_P01`, `D3_P02` (`HWAD1AR`) — patient-rights awareness and time-sensitive barriers in adult-service settings
- `D3_P09`, `D3_P11` (`HWAD4AR`) — inability to explain the pillars, shortage of time, and delayed appointments despite local awareness
- `D3_U02` (`HWAD6AR`) — language and communication barriers retained only as explicit `unclear` support, not as participant-count evidence

### Tensions within this theme
- Adults are told to ask questions and participate, yet hierarchy and defensiveness make questioning unevenly safe
- Holistic care is endorsed rhetorically, but everyday service structures still reward speed, triage, and throughput
- Health-literate providers can often navigate the system for themselves, but several segments explicitly note that ordinary patients or families cannot do the same

### Supporting codes
`trust_questioning_barrier`, `health_literacy_navigation`, `patient_provider_relationship`, `empathy_reassurance`, `consultation_time_pressure`, `authority_defensiveness`, `stigma_help_seeking_barrier`, `language_cultural_mismatch`, `fragmented_service_pathway`

## Theme 4: Coordinated, access-aware, and institutionally embedded routes to adult-care redesign

### Definition
When Day 3 participants move from critique into possibilities, they do not rely on vague calls for improvement. Their strongest proposals combine coordination across services, clearer access routes, public awareness, differentiated design, training, workforce support, policy review, and technology that reduces administrative drag or improves navigation. The central Day 3 logic is that adult wellbeing cannot be embedded in healthcare through goodwill alone. It has to be built into institutions, staffing, pathways, communication systems, and the practical organization of access.

### Key questions
- **Primary**: Q4-Q7
- **Secondary**: none — this theme is concentrated in the later challenge, opportunity, and recommendation sections

### Key participants and sources
- `D3_P19`, `D3_P20`, `D3_P21`, `D3_P22`, `D3_P24`, `D3_P25` (`HWAD10AR`) — collaboration, referral awareness, policy review, workforce support, and integration across services
- `D3_P09`, `D3_P11`, `D3_P14` (`HWAD4AR`) — campaigns, free counselling access, and local orientation/integration examples, with one close-reading support segment where theme fit exceeds the first-pass code assignment
- `D3_P01`, `D3_P02` (`HWAD1AR`) — patient-rights education, staffing, time, and coordination challenges feeding directly into redesign logic
- `D3_P05` plus `D3_U01` (`HWAD3AR`) — awareness and screening suggestions, with attribution caution maintained
- `D3_U02` (`HWAD6AR`) — explicit but attribution-cautious Q7 proposals on language access, evening clinics, time pressure, training, and one-visit integration

### Tensions within this theme
- Participants want top-down institutional change, but the same evidence base shows how time scarcity and staffing pressure can block implementation
- Technology is often proposed as relief, yet several segments imply that redesign must still protect relational care rather than just accelerate throughput
- Some of the strongest Q7 redesign material survives in `unclear` or moderator-shaped late sections, so the implementation theme is strong but unevenly attributable across sources

### Supporting codes
`interdisciplinary_integration`, `awareness_outreach`, `differentiated_service_design`, `digital_admin_relief`, `top_down_system_change`, `healthcare_worker_wellbeing`, `practical_recommendation`

## Cross-cutting pattern: Hidden strain behind adult competence and surface functioning

### Description
Across Day 3, participants repeatedly imply that adult distress can sit behind outward competence, responsibility, religious steadiness, or professional functioning. Adults may appear coping, productive, or socially present while still carrying unresolved strain, family tension, or burdens that do not show on the surface. This pattern strengthens Theme 1 by complicating any simple equation between outward functionality and wellbeing, strengthens Theme 2 by showing the burden hidden inside role performance, and strengthens Theme 3 by explaining why rushed or hierarchy-sensitive systems can miss what adults do not disclose openly.

### Evidence status
- Present across `HWAD10AR`, `HWAD3AR`, and parts of `HWAD4AR`
- Retained as a cross-cutting analytical pattern rather than a standalone final theme
- Interpreted with explicit caution because some of the clearest supporting lines come from `unclear` Day 3 material
