# CASE_D3 Language and Translation Memo

## Language composition
- **1 fully English transcript**: `HWAD10AR`
- **1 primarily Arabic transcript**: `HWAD1AR`
- **3 Arabic-dominant mixed-language transcripts**: `HWAD3AR`, `HWAD4AR`, `HWAD6AR`
- **Primarily Arabic note files**: `HWAD0NT1`, `HWAD1NT1`, `HWAD1NT2`, `HWAD3NT1`, `HWAD5NT1`, `HWAD7NT1`, `HWAD9NT1`
- **Primarily English note files**: `HWAD10NT1`, `HWAD10NTI`, `HWAD11NT2`, `HWAD11NT3`
- **Recommendation workbook**: `Health_Workshop_Suggestions Day 3.xlsx` treated as auxiliary structured recommendation material, not transcript-equivalent evidence

## Translation handling rules

### Arabic as authoritative where Arabic is original
Arabic is the authoritative source language for `HWAD1AR` and for the Arabic-dominant mixed transcripts `HWAD3AR`, `HWAD4AR`, and `HWAD6AR`. Where Arabic text is quoted or coded, the Arabic remains the primary evidentiary form. English glosses or translations are interpretive aids for reporting only.

### English sources are natively authoritative in their own right
`HWAD10AR` is a natively English transcript and should be quoted directly. The English note files remain authoritative as note-taker summaries of their own wording, but they are still note-derived contextual material rather than transcript speech.

### Mixed-language handling
Several Day 3 Arabic transcripts contain embedded English terms such as `wellbeing`, `balance`, `behavior`, `pathways`, `mental health`, `general practitioner`, `AI`, and similar professional or institutional vocabulary. These should not be normalised away during coding. The mixed-language form is part of how participants actually articulated adult-health concepts.

### Key Arabic concepts preserved
The following Arabic terms carry culturally and analytically important meaning and should be preserved in Arabic with glosses where needed:

| Arabic term | Transliteration | Working gloss | Why preserved |
|------------|----------------|---------------|---------------|
| الحياة الطيبة | al-hayat al-tayyiba | the good / wholesome life | Carries moral-spiritual and ethical resonance beyond a thin English wellbeing label |
| العافية | al-'afiya | wellness / wholeness / being well | Often exceeds narrow biomedical health and includes social, psychological, and moral dimensions |
| رضا | rida | acceptance / contentment | Often linked to moral stance and life orientation, not only subjective satisfaction |
| توازن | tawazun | balance | Recurrent adult-language anchor for integrated wellbeing |
| ركائز | raka'iz | pillars | Refers to the five HT pillars as an integrated framework |
| الراشدين | al-rashidin | adults | Important age/stakeholder marker in the Day 3 guide |
| المحاور | al-muhawir | interviewer / facilitator | Important moderator role label in Arabic sources |
| مدير الجلسة | mudir al-jalsa | session manager | Explicit moderator/facilitator role label |

### Note-file language asymmetry
The note files are not literal translations of transcript speech. Even where an English note file exists for an Arabic or mixed-language discussion, it must be treated as note-taker summary material rather than as an authoritative translation of participant wording.

### Recommendation workbook language handling
The recommendation workbook contains Arabic and English suggestions, but because it is auxiliary structured material rather than transcript evidence, language issues here are governed by the recommendation-usage rule rather than by quotation authority.

### Reporting convention
In later reporting outputs:
- Arabic quotations should be presented in Arabic with English translation or gloss where needed
- English quotations from `HWAD10AR` should be presented directly
- Mixed-language wording should be preserved where analytically meaningful
- The source language should be recorded for every quotation or excerpt used
- If an English note summary is used to support context for an Arabic source, it must be labeled as note-taker summary material rather than participant quotation
