# CASE_D3 Final Report: Day 3 — Adults

## Within-Case Reflexive Thematic Analysis

---

## 1. Introduction

This report presents the findings of a within-case reflexive thematic analysis of Day 3 (Adults) focus group discussions from the Health and Wellbeing Workshop series. Across the Day 3 corpus, participants move repeatedly between expansive definitions of adult wellbeing and concrete descriptions of healthcare systems that often make that ideal difficult to sustain. The adult case is especially marked by the intertwining of balance, moral-spiritual grounding, family and work responsibilities, time pressure, communication barriers, and institution-level redesign talk.

### 1.1 Scope and boundaries
- **Case**: `CASE_D3` only. No cross-case comparisons.
- **Sources**: 5 transcripts (`HWAD1AR`, `HWAD3AR`, `HWAD4AR`, `HWAD6AR`, `HWAD10AR`), 11 note files, and 1 auxiliary recommendation workbook.
- **Participants**: 25 confirmed participants (`D3_P01–D3_P25`), 10 moderators (`D3_M01–D3_M10`), and 2 register-level `unclear` speaker rows.
- **Structured coded base**: 1,234 coded segments = 489 participant + 307 moderator + 438 `unclear`.
- **Discussion structure**: Seven questions (`Q1–Q7`) spanning conceptual understandings of adult wellbeing (Q1-Q3) and healthcare reality, barriers, opportunities, and implementation suggestions (Q4-Q7).
- **Analytical approach**: Reflexive thematic analysis with explicit `speaker_type` distinction maintained throughout (`participant`, `moderator`, `unclear`, `note_taker_summary`).

### 1.2 Methodological notes
- Arabic remains the authoritative language for `HWAD1AR` and the Arabic-dominant mixed transcripts `HWAD3AR`, `HWAD4AR`, and `HWAD6AR`. English quotations from `HWAD10AR` are quoted directly.
- Moderator speech is excluded from participant evidence counts, quotation diversity, matrix counts, theme support, and prominence support.
- No `note_taker_summary` rows were operationalized in the final Day 3 outward-facing evidence layer. Day 3 note files remain contextual references only and are not quoted here as participant evidence.
- The recommendation workbook remains auxiliary structured material only. It does not generate themes and is not treated here as transcript-equivalent participant evidence.
- Four Day 3 source limitations remain explicit throughout this report: weak `HWAD1AR` `Q2/Q3` boundaries, weak `HWAD10AR` `Q6` preservation, later moderator-led summary structure, and substantial `unclear` material in `HWAD3AR` and `HWAD6AR`.

### 1.3 Evidence classification and quotation provenance rule
This report keeps four evidence/support categories explicit:
1. **Coded transcript support** — excerpt-bank rows that directly match the Day 3 interpretive theme rules.
2. **Close-reading support** — transcript rows retained in the excerpt bank because they are analytically important to later interpretation even where the first-pass code assignment underindexes the theme claim.
3. **Contextual note support** — not operationalized in the final Day 3 outward-facing evidence layer.
4. **Auxiliary recommendation support** — not used as theme-generating evidence and not quoted here as participant speech.

**Rule**: Every quotation in this report includes its support label and evidence identifier (for example `D3_S0004`). Quotation traceability is therefore directly testable against `CASE_D3_excerpt_bank.csv`.

### 1.4 Coding support-layer note
The coded segment dataset, excerpt bank, question-theme matrix, and prominence tables support traceability, matrix building, and evidence organisation, but they are not the primary theme-generation engine for this report. Themes were developed through close reading, contextual interpretation, memo-based reasoning, question-context analysis, source-sensitivity review, and tension analysis. The structured layer is therefore a transparency and checking mechanism rather than a substitute for interpretive theme development.

Four final themes emerged from the Day 3 data, plus one cross-cutting analytical pattern:

### Salience logic
Theme 1 is the most prominent because it anchors the conceptual frame through which participants judge later service realities. Theme 2 is highly prominent because family, work, caregiving, role burden, and the negotiation between self-responsibility and system responsibility recur across the early and middle questions. Theme 3 is moderately prominent because its evidence is dense and analytically important but more concentrated in the service/barrier part of the discussion. Theme 4 is present but less prominent because it is strongly supported in the later questions but is more focused than Themes 1 and 2 in its spread across the full case.

## 3. Question-by-Question Analysis

### 3.1 Q1: What is wellbeing for you? (ما معنى الحياة الطيبة بالنسبة لك؟)

**78 participant segments, 22 participant speakers, 5 transcript sources**

Q1 establishes the Day 3 case as conceptually broad from the outset. Participants do not define adult wellbeing as simple happiness or symptom absence. Instead, they repeatedly describe it through balance, coping, internal steadiness, relational positioning, and moral-spiritual orientation.

> "To me, well-being is really all about feeling reasonably content and able to cope with the stressors... despite all of that, having the happy Tuesdays and the happy Wednesdays, that is well-being for me." — D3_P19, `HWAD10AR`, Table 10 `[coded transcript support, D3_S0004]`

> "الصراحة اليوم أستطيع أن أقول أن فعلا أنا أشعر بالحياة الطيبة... لكن بين الروحية والعاطفية فيه اضطراب كبير، فعندما حققت التوازن الحمد لله، هنا صار هناك حياة طيبة." — D3_P05, `HWAD3AR`, Table 3 `[coded transcript support, D3_S0496]`

The bilingual spread matters. In the English material, wellbeing is tied to coping and livable continuity under stress. In the Arabic material, it is tied not only to balance but also to what kind of life feels morally, spiritually, and emotionally settled from within. Q1 therefore lays the foundation for Theme 1 and also starts Theme 2, because adults often define being well in relation to the roles and pressures they must carry.

**Analytical significance**: Later criticism of healthcare is not free-floating dissatisfaction. It is measured against an early, richly layered account of what adult wellbeing should look like.

### 3.2 Q2: How do you define wellness from your perspective? When we say someone is “well,” what do we mean? (كيف تعرّف العافية من منظورك الشخصي؟ عندما نقول هذا الشخص "بعافية" ماذا نقصد؟)

**81 participant segments, 14 participant speakers, 3 transcript sources**

Q2 sharpens the adult meaning of wellness. Adults are not described as well simply because they appear functional on the surface. Instead, participants move toward responsibility, value orientation, social role performance, and the ability to hold one’s life together across multiple obligations.

> "I think it's about appreciating where you are at and doing whatever you can... And I think that's an adult's responsibility." — D3_P22, `HWAD10AR`, Table 10 `[coded transcript support, D3_S0067]`

> "اللهم إني أسألك العفو والعافية في ديني ودنياي وأهلي ومالي" — D3_P16, `HWAD4AR`, Table 4 `[coded transcript support, D3_S0793]`

Q2 is narrower in source spread than Q1, and the weak `HWAD1AR` `Q2/Q3` boundary remains important here. The strongest Q2 evidence comes from `HWAD10AR` and `HWAD4AR`, not from assuming that every early Day 3 source preserves the question equally cleanly. Even so, the adult logic is clear: wellness is judged through the fit between inner orientation and outer responsibilities rather than through bodily appearance alone.

**Analytical significance**: Q2 strengthens Theme 1 and Theme 2 together. It also sets up the cross-cutting hidden-strain pattern by showing why visible adult functioning is an incomplete indicator of how well someone actually is.

### 3.3 Q3: Based on the five HT pillars, how can these pillars contribute to improving your wellbeing? (بناء على منظور الحياة الطيبة وركائزها الخمسة، كيف يمكن لهذه الركائز أن تسهم في تحسين عافيتك؟)

**96 participant segments, 16 participant speakers, 4 transcript sources**

Q3 is where Day 3 moves most clearly from abstract definition into action logic. Participants do not speak only about adding more pillars into their lives. They repeatedly move between self-work, family obligation, and the argument that adult wellbeing cannot be improved by individual effort alone if wider systems remain unchanged.

> "هو فعلا يبتدي من المسؤولية مسؤوليتي تجاه نفسي... اذا ابتديت بنفسي يكون عندي وقت اني يعني التفت لاولادي بعد التفت لاسرتي اذا هناك مشاكل سحقق العافية ان شاء الله." — D3_P05, `HWAD3AR`, Table 3 `[coded transcript support, D3_S0560]`

> "Yes, we should think individual, but we should think systems... عندما يحدث أي شيء نحمله هو المسؤولية إلى آخره، نهمل الsystems." — D3_U01, `HWAD3AR`, Table 3 `[coded transcript support from an unclear row, D3_S0562]`

Q3 therefore does important interpretive work. It strengthens Theme 2 by showing that adults think of wellbeing through family, work, and responsibility. At the same time, it prevents that theme from collapsing into individual blame by insisting on system responsibility. Because `HWAD1AR` preserves Q3 less cleanly than `HWAD3AR`, `HWAD4AR`, and `HWAD10AR`, the strongest Day 3 Q3 interpretation rests on those cleaner sources.

**Analytical significance**: Q3 is the clearest bridge between conceptual wellbeing, lived adult strain, and later implementation talk.

### 3.4 Q4: Based on your experience, are the HT pillars reflected in healthcare services currently provided to adults? (هل تنعكس ركائز الحياة الطيبة في خدمات الرعاية الصحية المقدمة حاليا إلى فئة الراشدين؟)

**106 participant segments, 16 participant speakers, 5 transcript sources**

Q4 turns the discussion from ideal to healthcare reality. Participants do not usually say that the pillars are absent in an absolute sense. Instead, they say that elements are present but unevenly enacted, weakly integrated, or squeezed by time, throughput, and service design.

> "They said to us, the physicians or the medical team helps us get physically better... But when we leave the facility, how are we living? How is our well-being?" — D3_P20, `HWAD10AR`, Table 10 `[coded transcript support, D3_S0225]`

> "Only one thing... getting an appointment within a time when we are sick is a task." — D3_P24, `HWAD10AR`, Table 10 `[coded transcript support, D3_S0241]`

These Q4 claims are not only complaints about convenience. They show the specific conditions under which adults can or cannot encounter whole-person care. Q4 is therefore the strongest early entry point into Theme 3 and Theme 4: healthcare may contain pieces of the pillars, but adults still experience the system as uneven in access, pace, explanation, and coordination.

**Analytical significance**: Q4 is the point where the adult case stops being primarily conceptual and becomes an interpretation of current service conditions.

### 3.5 Q5: What are the main challenges in integrating the HT pillars into healthcare services? (ما التحديات الرئيسية التي تواجه دمج ركائز الحياة الطيبة ضمن خدمات الرعاية الصحية؟)

**90 participant segments, 20 participant speakers, 5 transcript sources**

Q5 concentrates the challenge layer. The barriers are not described as merely logistical. Participants repeatedly connect them to rights awareness, the safety of questioning providers, explanation quality, staffing pressure, and the difficulty of making holistic care understandable and accessible to ordinary adult patients and families.

> "التحدي الأكبر هو تحدي الوعي والتثقيف للمرضى بحقوقهم والكادر الطبي ايضا بحقوق المرضى." — D3_P01, `HWAD1AR`, Table 1 `[coded transcript support, D3_S0464]`

> "You're challenging my authority or you're challenging my decision. I've had situations... in which I've gone to an appointment and I have asked questions of the provider." — D3_P20, `HWAD10AR`, Table 10 `[coded transcript support, D3_S0338]`

An important Arabic echo appears in `HWAD4AR`, where one participant says that the specialist "لا يعرف يشرح five different aspects" (`D3_S0851`), reinforcing the same problem from another source: explanation and integration fail precisely where adults need more orientation, not less. Q5 therefore consolidates Theme 3 and strengthens Theme 4 because the challenge talk is already framed as a design and system problem rather than only a personal one.

**Analytical significance**: Q5 is where Day 3 moves from broad dissatisfaction into a more specific critique of hierarchy, explanation, time, and navigation.

### 3.6 Q6: What opportunities are currently available to enhance integration of the HT pillars into healthcare practices? (ما الفرص المتاحة حاليا لتعزيز دمج ركائز الحياة الطيبة في ممارسات الرعاية الصحية؟)

**23 participant segments, 11 participant speakers, 4 transcript sources**

Q6 is analytically useful but methodologically uneven. The weak `HWAD10AR` `Q6` preservation means the strongest direct Q6 opportunity material comes from `HWAD4AR` and narrower supporting material elsewhere. Even so, participants identify existing openings rather than speaking only in abstract aspirations.

> "الفرصة الكبيرة التي نراها ما شاء الله عندنا موجودة هنا نحن في جامعة قطر نوفر psycho therapy counseling for free." — D3_P11, `HWAD4AR`, Table 4 `[close-reading support, D3_S0863]`

> "أنا أحس أنه لازم يكون في proper strong campaigns like breast cancer campaigns." — D3_P09, `HWAD4AR`, Table 4 `[coded transcript support, D3_S0868]`

Q6 opportunity logic therefore runs through access that already exists, awareness mechanisms that could be strengthened, and orientation or training structures that could embed the pillars more consistently. The available opportunity layer is real, but it is not evenly preserved across all Day 3 sources and should not be presented as if every table contributed equally clean Q6 material.

**Analytical significance**: Q6 strengthens Theme 4, but only with explicit caution around the uneven preservation of late-question material.

### 3.7 Q7: What practical suggestions do you have for integrating the HT pillars to improve healthcare services? (ما هي اقتراحاتكم العملية لطرق دمج ركائز الحياة الطيبة لتحسين خدمات الرعاية الصحية؟)

**15 participant segments, 9 participant speakers, 5 transcript sources, plus substantial attribution-uncertain late discussion**

Q7 contains some of the most concrete Day 3 redesign thinking, but it is also where the Day 3 attribution problem becomes most visible. The strongest surviving practical-suggestion material comes from `HWAD6AR`, where late discussion is substantial but largely preserved as `unclear` rather than cleanly speaker-attributed transcript turns.

> "تكرارا وتكرارا... كيف تحل أزمة الوقت؟ ... الدكتور يقول أنا ما عندي وقت." — D3_U02, `HWAD6AR`, Table 6 `[coded transcript support from an unclear row, D3_S1149]`

> "دمج الركائز الخمسة للحياة الطيبة بشكل متكامل في الخدمات الصحية... وإدماج الركائز الخمسة للحياة الطيبة ضمن تكوين وتدريب الكوادر الطبية والشبه الطبية... وتجديد التراخيص المهنية." — D3_U02, `HWAD6AR`, Table 6 `[close-reading support from an unclear row, D3_S1153]`

These rows are analytically strong because they move directly into redesign: time, integration, training, and licensing. But they are also attribution-cautious. They support the argument that Day 3 participants were thinking institutionally and practically in the later discussion, while also showing why the final Day 3 report must not pretend that Q7 is as cleanly attributable as the earlier English or Arabic participant-coded sections.

**Analytical significance**: Q7 sharpens Theme 4 and makes the Day 3 evidence boundary itself visible. The redesign logic is strong, but the final suggestion layer is more attribution-uneven than the early conceptual layer.

## 4. Thematic Analysis

### 4.1 Theme 1: Adult wellbeing as integrated balance, moral-spiritual grounding, and workable coping

**Salience: Most prominent** — 143 coded participant segments, 21 speakers, 4 tables.

Theme 1 is the conceptual foundation of the Day 3 case. Participants repeatedly describe adult wellbeing as an integrated condition: balance across domains, moral-spiritual orientation, contentment, relationship quality, and the practical ability to cope. The adult case is distinctive because it repeatedly links these elements to how adults carry themselves under pressure rather than to abstract wellness ideals alone.

**Why this matters**: Later critiques of healthcare are measured against this earlier integrated model. When participants say services are rushed, fragmented, or overly physicalized, they are judging them against a prior understanding of adult wellbeing as broader, deeper, and more relationally situated than symptom management.

### 4.2 Theme 2: Adult wellbeing as negotiated through family, work, caregiving, and role-bearing strain

**Salience: Highly prominent** — 121 coded participant segments, 15 speakers, 4 tables.

Theme 2 captures how strongly adulthood is framed through obligation. Participants speak about children, families, work, caseloads, social duty, self-management, and the burden of remaining functional for others. Just as importantly, they refuse to reduce adult wellbeing to individual discipline alone. Day 3 repeatedly insists that adults are judged within systems that shape what they can sustain.

**Why this matters**: Theme 2 gives the adult case its life-stage specificity. Day 3 is not simply a generic wellbeing discussion moved one day later. It is a case about responsibility-bearing adults trying to hold together inner steadiness, family roles, and institutional demand.

### 4.3 Theme 3: Holistic adult care undermined by time pressure, hierarchy, navigation gaps, and uneven communication

**Salience: Moderately prominent** — 103 coded participant segments, 19 speakers, 4 tables.

Theme 3 is the core critique of current care conditions. The adult case repeatedly describes care environments where people are rushed, uncertain about routes, hesitant to question authority, or unable to obtain clear explanation in a linguistically and culturally comfortable form. The central issue is not only efficiency failure. It is the weakening of the conditions under which adults can participate meaningfully in their own care.

**Why this matters**: Theme 3 explains why the pillars may be endorsed in principle but still fail to become lived healthcare realities. Adults do not only need services to exist; they need those services to be navigable, explainable, and relationally safe.

### 4.4 Theme 4: Coordinated, access-aware, and institutionally embedded routes to adult-care redesign

**Salience: Present but less prominent** — 95 coded participant segments, 18 speakers, 5 tables.

Theme 4 gathers the most concrete redesign logic in the case: awareness campaigns, free access points, training, integration, better communication structures, differentiated pathways, time relief, and policy-level embedding. What ties these suggestions together is the claim that adult wellbeing cannot be integrated into healthcare through goodwill alone. It has to be organized into systems.

**Why this matters**: Theme 4 makes Day 3 future-facing without turning it into a recommendation workbook summary. The redesign logic comes from the transcript layer itself, while the recommendation workbook remains auxiliary only.

### 4.5 Cross-cutting pattern: Hidden strain behind adult competence and surface functioning

Across the Day 3 case, adults are repeatedly understood as capable of looking functional while carrying unresolved strain, moral conflict, family burden, or private distress. This pattern strengthens Theme 1 by complicating any simple equation between outward functioning and wellbeing. It strengthens Theme 2 by showing the burden hidden within role performance, and it strengthens Theme 3 by explaining why rushed or hierarchy-sensitive care can miss what adults do not disclose openly.

## 5. Tensions and Contradictions

1. **Integrated wellbeing ideal vs. pressured service reality**: Participants define adult wellbeing broadly, then describe healthcare systems that make whole-person care difficult to deliver.
2. **Self-responsibility vs. system responsibility**: Adults are repeatedly urged to regulate themselves, yet participants insist that institutions should not offload structural failure onto individuals.
3. **Family as support vs. family as burden**: Family appears as a source of grounding, but also as a site of strain, silence, risk, and obligation.
4. **Provider expertise vs. provider vulnerability**: Many of the clearest Day 3 voices are professionals, but they also describe themselves as adults under pressure rather than detached experts outside the problem.
5. **Strong redesign logic vs. weak late-question attribution**: The later implementation and practical-suggestion material is analytically rich, but some of its strongest lines survive in `unclear` or moderator-shaped late transcript structure.

---

## 6. Methodological Limitations

- **Weak `HWAD1AR` `Q2/Q3` boundaries**: Early Day 3 Arabic material in `HWAD1AR` does not preserve a fully clean boundary between those questions, so later interpretation should not overclaim question-specific precision from that source.
- **Weak `HWAD10AR` `Q6` preservation**: The strongest English source remains highly productive, but it does not preserve a clean standalone `Q6` section in the current extraction.
- **Later moderator-led summary structure**: Especially in later recommendation-oriented discussion, some material survives primarily as moderator-shaped contextual structure and cannot be treated as participant evidence.
- **Substantial `unclear` material in `HWAD3AR` and `HWAD6AR`**: These sources remain analytically important, but attribution is uneven and cannot be smoothed over in participant-diversity or prominence claims.
- **Professional voice dominance**: The cleanest evidence still comes disproportionately from professional and provider positions, even though the case is explicitly about adult wellbeing more broadly.
- **No note-taker summaries operationalized in the final outward-facing evidence layer**: Note files remain contextual only in the Day 3 final package.
- **Recommendation workbook remains auxiliary only**: It was not used to generate themes and does not function here as transcript-equivalent evidence.
