# CASE_D2 Candidate Theme Development

## Development process
Candidate themes were developed through iterative engagement with the reviewed Day 2 evidence base:
1. Familiarisation across the Day 2 transcript and note corpus with source-sensitivity constraints recorded before theme work
2. Review of the tightened `CASE_D2_coded_segments.csv` layer and its linked Gate 3 summary artifacts
3. Code clustering by analytic function, question spread, participant spread, and source sensitivity rather than keyword repetition alone
4. Boundary-testing against the participant-coded question-theme matrix, the outward-facing excerpt bank, and explicit note-provenance rules

## Candidate themes tested

### Candidate A — Multidimensional wellbeing as balance, contentment, support, and workable daily functioning
- **Status**: RETAINED as Theme 1
- **Core idea**: Participants define youth wellbeing and wellness as a multidimensional state rather than the absence of illness. The strongest Day 2 formulation combines balance across the pillars, contentment and acceptance, moral-spiritual steadiness, social support, and the practical ability to function in everyday life. The theme is not only conceptual: participants repeatedly connect the idea of being well to coping, productivity, and staying connected to other people.
- **Supporting codes**: `balance_multidimensional` (38), `support_as_wellbeing_core` (22), `spiritual_moral_anchor` (21), `contentment_acceptance` (12), `functionality_self_management` (11), `inner_peace_stability` (7)
- **Evidence spread**: Q1-Q3 primary; echoes continue through Q4-Q7. Strongest support from Tables 0, 9, and 11, with supporting conceptual material from Tables 4 and 10.
- **Analytical sharpness**: This is not a generic "wellbeing means many things" theme. It captures a distinct Day 2 logic: wellbeing is simultaneously moral, relational, embodied, and functional.

### Candidate B — Youth wellbeing as a relational ecology of family, peers, digital life, and social visibility
- **Status**: RETAINED as Theme 2
- **Core idea**: Participants rarely describe youth wellbeing as an individual attribute detached from context. Instead, they locate it in social environments: family presence or absence, peer belonging, social isolation inside the home, digital overexposure, and the difficulty of making youth needs visible to adults and institutions. The recurring Day 2 concern is not merely stress, but socially produced disconnection.
- **Supporting codes**: `family_support_ecology` (21), `peer_belonging_isolation` (11), `digital_social_media_pressure` (5), `youth_transition_pressure` (3), `youth_voice_visibility` (1)
- **Evidence spread**: Q3 primary; Q1-Q2 establish the relational framing and Q4-Q7 carry it into care, school, and implementation discussion. Strongest support from Tables 0, 9, and 11, with supplementary youth-service material from Tables 4 and 10.
- **Analytical sharpness**: This theme captures the youth case as socially situated. It is not about "family support" in isolation, but about how youth wellbeing is made or broken across households, peers, school settings, and digital life.

### Candidate C — Hidden distress and the unreliability of surface functioning
- **Status**: ABSORBED as cross-cutting analytical pattern
- **Core idea**: Across several Day 2 sources, participants warn that distress is often concealed behind apparently normal functioning, routine social presentation, or physical complaints. Youth may appear well, productive, or socially present while remaining emotionally distressed, reluctant to disclose, or unable to name what is wrong.
- **Supporting codes**: `self_disclosure_hidden_distress` (5), `stigma_help_seeking_barrier` (7), `confidentiality_trust_barrier` (6)
- **Evidence spread**: Q2, Q4, and Q5 strongest, with echoes in adolescent-access discussion and recommendation material.
- **Reason for absorption**: The pattern is analytically important, but the Day 2 evidence shows it operating inside both the youth social ecology theme and the constrained-care theme rather than standing cleanly on its own.

### Candidate D — Holistic care is constrained by rushed, fragmented, and trust-sensitive healthcare encounters
- **Status**: RETAINED as Theme 3
- **Core idea**: Participants describe a sharp mismatch between the holistic ideal of youth wellbeing and the conditions under which care is actually delivered. Disclosure is hard, time is short, waiting is long, language and culture can obstruct trust, and service structures often encourage problem-specific treatment over whole-person care. The result is not only inconvenience but a patterned weakening of youth-supportive care.
- **Supporting codes**: `patient_provider_relationship` (11), `access_delay_waiting` (8), `stigma_help_seeking_barrier` (7), `confidentiality_trust_barrier` (6), `consultation_time_pressure` (5), `language_cultural_mismatch` (5), `self_disclosure_hidden_distress` (5), `empathy_reassurance` (4), `whole_person_care` (3), `fragmented_service_pathway` (1), `environment_design_stress` (1)
- **Evidence spread**: Q4-Q6 primary, with conceptual preparation in Q1-Q3 and lingering implications in Q7. Strongest support from Tables 0, 9, 10, and 11.
- **Analytical sharpness**: This is not a loose list of barriers. It captures how trust, time, disclosure, and coordination problems specifically undermine attempts to treat youth wellbeing holistically.

### Candidate E — Coordinated, youth-sensitive, and institutionally backed implementation as the route to change
- **Status**: RETAINED as Theme 4
- **Core idea**: When Day 2 participants move into opportunities and recommendations, they do not rely on vague calls for improvement. Their strongest proposals combine coordination across settings, youth-specific service design, staff training, family inclusion, staff wellbeing, and leadership-backed implementation. The consistent logic is that change must be designed into institutions, not left to individual goodwill.
- **Supporting codes**: `top_down_system_change` (17), `interdisciplinary_integration` (11), `communication_training_need` (6), `healthcare_worker_wellbeing` (5), `youth_specific_service_design` (5), `practical_recommendation` (5), `awareness_outreach` (3), `family_inclusive_care` (1)
- **Evidence spread**: Q4-Q7 primary; Q3 offers some early institutional framing. Strongest support from Tables 9, 10, and 11, with supporting policy and implementation material from Tables 0 and 4.
- **Analytical sharpness**: Day 2 change talk is concrete: school nurses, counsellors, staff support, mandated structures, unified training, social-media campaigns, and systems that are designed for adolescents rather than retrofitted to them.

### Candidate F — Professional mediation of youth voice
- **Status**: NOT a theme — documented as methodological limitation
- **Core idea**: The case concerns youth wellbeing, but much of the cleanest material is voiced by clinicians, academics, and service professionals speaking about youth rather than by young people speaking at length in their own right.
- **Reason for exclusion**: This is a property of the dataset and source recoverability, not a participant-generated substantive pattern. It should be acknowledged in reporting but not elevated into a final theme.

## Final theme count: 4 themes + 1 cross-cutting pattern
