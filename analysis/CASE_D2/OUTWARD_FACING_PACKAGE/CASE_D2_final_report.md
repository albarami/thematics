# CASE_D2 Final Report: Day 2 — Youth Wellbeing

## Within-Case Reflexive Thematic Analysis

---

## 1. Introduction

This report presents the findings of a within-case reflexive thematic analysis of Day 2 (Youth) focus group discussions from the Health and Wellbeing Workshop series. Day 2 convened stakeholders concerned with youth wellbeing across healthcare, psychology, education, research, human resources, and lived-service contexts. The strongest Day 2 pattern is a repeated movement between defining wellbeing in broad, morally and relationally grounded terms and then describing how actual service conditions make that ideal difficult to realise for young people.

### 1.1 Scope and boundaries
- **Case**: `CASE_D2` only. No cross-case comparisons.
- **Sources**: 7 transcripts (`HWYO0AR`, `HWYO3AR`, `HWYO4AR`, `HWYO7AR`, `HWYO9AR`, `HWYO10AR`, `HWYO11AR`), 10 note-taker files, 1 auxiliary recommendation workbook.
- **Participants**: 38 confirmed participants (`D2_P01–D2_P38` in contiguous outward-facing numbering), 10 moderators (`D2_M01–D2_M10`), and 2 register-level unclear speaker rows across 7 tables.
- **Structured coded base**: 321 coded segments = 212 participant + 92 moderator + 17 unclear.
- **Discussion structure**: Seven questions (`Q1–Q7`) spanning conceptual understanding of wellbeing (Q1-Q3) and healthcare reality, challenges, opportunities, and practical suggestions (Q4-Q7).
- **Analytical approach**: Reflexive thematic analysis with explicit `speaker_type` distinction maintained throughout (`participant`, `moderator`, `unclear`, `note_taker_summary`).

### 1.2 Methodological notes
- Arabic remains the authoritative language for the Arabic transcripts and note files. Key Arabic concepts are preserved in the report rather than flattened into English-only abstraction.
- Moderator speech is excluded from participant evidence counts, quotation diversity metrics, matrix counts, and theme support.
- `HWYO10AR` is retained as transcript evidence, but because the source behaves partly like compressed, note-style English Q&A, quotations from it are explicitly labeled as `[note-style transcript summary]`.
- `note_taker_summary` material is operationalized in the outward-facing excerpt bank and may appear in this report as explicitly labeled contextual support. It is not counted as participant evidence and does not drive the question-theme matrix or prominence layer.
- The recommendation workbook remains auxiliary structured recommendation material only. It is not used to generate themes and is not treated as transcript-equivalent participant evidence.
- The Day 2 corpus includes visible student and service-recipient positions, but provider and professional voices still dominate the cleanest evidentiary material.

### 1.3 Evidence classification and quotation provenance rule
This report uses four categories of evidence, each labeled at point of use:
1. **Verbatim transcript quotation** — direct speech from transcript material.
2. **Note-style transcript summary** — structured transcript material from `HWYO10AR` that behaves more like compressed summary than turn-by-turn verbatim speech.
3. **Note-taker summary** — explicitly labeled contextual support from note files, preserved in the excerpt bank as `note_taker_summary` rows.
4. **Auxiliary recommendation reference** — structured workbook material used only if explicitly marked `[auxiliary]`.

**Rule**: Every quotation in this report is tied to a documented evidence row and includes its evidence type and evidence identifier (for example `D2_S0011` or `D2_E001`). This makes quotation traceability directly testable against `CASE_D2_excerpt_bank.csv`.

### 1.4 Coding support-layer note
The coded segment dataset and linked tables support traceability, matrix building, and evidence organisation, but they are not the primary theme-generation engine for this report. Themes were developed through close reading, contextual interpretation, memo-based reasoning, question-context interpretation, source-sensitivity review, and tension analysis. The matrix and salience layer are therefore supplementary transparency structures rather than substitutes for interpretive theme development.

## 2. Themes Overview

Four final themes emerged from the Day 2 data, plus one cross-cutting analytical pattern:

| # | Theme | Salience | Coded segments | Speakers | Tables |
|---|-------|----------|----------------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Multidimensional wellbeing as balanced stability, moral-spiritual grounding, and daily functioning | Most prominent | 83 | 27 | 6 |
| 2 | Youth wellbeing as relational ecology, peer worlds, and social disconnection | Moderately prominent | 32 | 21 | 6 |
| 3 | Holistic care constrained by rushed, fragmented, and trust-sensitive encounters | Highly prominent | 44 | 22 | 6 |
| 4 | Coordinated, youth-sensitive, and institutionally backed routes to change | Present but less prominent | 40 | 18 | 5 |
| — | *Cross-cutting: Hidden distress and the unreliability of surface functioning* | *Analytically significant* | *—* | *—* | *—* |

### Salience logic
Theme 1 is the most prominent because it appears across all seven questions and functions as the conceptual frame through which participants judge later service realities. Theme 3 is highly prominent because it combines broad question coverage with strong evidence density around trust, time, and disclosure. Theme 2 is moderately prominent because it remains widespread but more concentrated around the relational and social framing of youth life. Theme 4 has a large evidence base, but its support is concentrated in Q3-Q7 and is therefore analytically focused rather than case-wide in the same way as Theme 1.

## 3. Question-by-Question Analysis

### 3.1 Q1: What is wellbeing for you? (ما معنى الحياة الطيبة بالنسبة لك؟)

**26 participant segments, 22 speakers, 5 transcript sources**

Q1 establishes the Day 2 case as conceptually broad from the outset. Participants do not define wellbeing narrowly as comfort or absence of illness. Instead, they repeatedly describe it through contentment, balance, relational support, and moral orientation.

> "الحياة طيبة يعني كما تفضل الإخوة الكرام الأعزاء تعني الرضا، هل أنت راضي عن ذاتك؟ تعني أن تكون مفيدا لنفسك ولغيرك." — D2_P05, HWYO0AR, Table 0 `[verbatim transcript, D2_S0011]`

> "جوهر الحياة الطيبة هو التوازن بين الجوانب الجسدية، النفسية، العقلية، الروحية، والاجتماعية." — HWYO5NT1, Table 5 `[note-taker summary, D2_E001]`

The note-supported balance formulation does not replace transcript evidence; it confirms how consistently participants were framing the concept across sources. Q1 therefore lays the groundwork for Theme 1 and anticipates Theme 2 by showing that wellbeing is already understood as socially and morally embedded.

**Analytical significance**: The youth case begins from an aspirational definition of a workable, balanced life. This matters because later criticism of services is not free-floating dissatisfaction; it is measured against a richly defined ideal.

### 3.2 Q2: How do you define wellness? (كيف تعرّف العافية من منظورك الشخصي؟)

**45 participant segments, 24 speakers, 5 transcript sources**

Q2 deepens the conceptual framing by distinguishing wellness from simple physical health. Participants repeatedly stress functioning, productivity, emotional steadiness, and the ability to keep going through ordinary life.

> "For me, I think it's about functioning again, right? So if you are able to get up in the morning and do everything that you need to do..." — D2_P15, HWYO9AR, Table 9 `[verbatim transcript, D2_S0255]`

> "لما أنا أفكر بعافيتي أفكر هل أنا منتجة بالمجتمع هل أنا نفسيا مرتاحة وعندي علاقات اجتماعية" — D2_P38, HWYO11AR, Table 11 `[verbatim transcript, D2_S0131]`

Q2 therefore strengthens Theme 1 but also prepares the cross-cutting hidden-distress pattern. If wellness is judged partly through visible functioning, then cases of hidden suffering become analytically important. This also helps explain why some later challenge talk centers on what people do not disclose.

### 3.3 Q3: How can the pillars contribute to improving wellbeing? (كيف يمكن للركائز أن تسهم في تحسين العافية؟)

**43 participant segments, 19 speakers, 5 transcript sources**

Q3 is where Day 2 moves most clearly from abstract definition into youth-specific social interpretation. The dominant move here is relational: participants talk about youth in families, with peers, in schools, and inside digital environments.

> "العزلة في مفهومنا الحديث لا تعني غياب العائلة أو الوالدين، بل تعني أن يعيش الفرد داخل أسرته ومع ذلك يظل معزولاً." — D2_P04, HWYO0AR, Table 0 `[verbatim transcript, D2_S0028]`

> "The social pillar is the most important for young people because you cannot do anything alone." — D2_P24, HWYO10AR, Table 10 `[note-style transcript summary, D2_S0082]`

This question is where Theme 2 becomes clearest. Youth wellbeing is repeatedly framed not as a private internal condition but as something shaped by support, isolation, communication, and the quality of surrounding relationships. Q3 also shows the first clear link between the conceptual pillars and concrete institutions like families and schools.

### 3.4 Q4: Do the pillars currently appear in healthcare services? (هل تنعكس الركائز في خدمات الرعاية الصحية المقدمة حالياً؟)

**44 participant segments, 19 speakers, 4 transcript sources**

Q4 turns the discussion from ideal to reality. Participants acknowledge that elements of the pillars do appear in practice, but they repeatedly qualify that recognition by describing care as uneven, pressured, and incomplete.

> "لكن (التعاطف الوجداني) مع المريض وأسرته هو ما سقط من حساباتنا كأطباء." — D2_P02, HWYO0AR, Table 0 `[verbatim transcript, D2_S0036]`

> "The pressure of having 30 patients waiting outside damages the image of healthcare professionals because they cannot spend enough time with each person." — D2_P24, HWYO10AR, Table 10 `[note-style transcript summary, D2_S0089]`

Q4 is the strongest early entry point into Theme 3. Even where participants defend parts of the system, they describe conditions that make whole-person, youth-sensitive care difficult to sustain. Q4 also begins Theme 4 because many "reality" observations are already expressed as implementation problems requiring institutional response.

### 3.5 Q5: What challenges face youth wellbeing in healthcare? (ما التحديات التي تواجه دمج هذه الركائز في الرعاية الصحية؟)

**30 participant segments, 18 speakers, 5 transcript sources**

Q5 concentrates the challenge layer. The main barriers are not described as purely technical; they are trust-sensitive, communicative, cultural, and temporal.

> "فعندما تُفصح المريضة للطبيبة عن معاناتها مع الاكتئاب، فإنها تمنحها ثقتها بناءً على موافقة ضمنية بأن تظل هذه المعلومة سرية" — D2_P04, HWYO0AR, Table 0 `[verbatim transcript, D2_S0046]`

> "Yes, basically when the people come, most of the time they are not physically unwell... they just express as a physical problem." — D2_P22, HWYO9AR, Table 9 `[verbatim transcript, D2_S0285]`

Day 2 challenge talk therefore does two things at once. First, it sharpens Theme 3 by showing how confidentiality, time pressure, language mismatch, and weak relational continuity distort care. Second, it strengthens the hidden-distress pattern by showing that distress may be misread or rerouted into bodily complaint when people do not feel safe enough to speak directly.

### 3.6 Q6: What opportunities exist to improve integration? (ما الفرص المتاحة حالياً لتعزيز الدمج؟)

**14 participant segments, 11 participant speakers, 11 unclear segments, 3 transcript sources**

Q6 shifts the case toward structured opportunity. Participants identify openings that already exist inside schools, health facilities, frontline teams, and institutional design. The key move is to locate practical entry points rather than abstract wishes.

> "School nurses should ideally be trained more than just somebody who wipes the kid's nose when they have a sniffle." — D2_P22, HWYO9AR, Table 9 `[verbatim transcript, D2_S0304]`

> "The first assessment tool is being ruled out in school. Parenting the parents by teaching them." — HWYO9NT1[7], Table 9 `[note-taker summary, D2_E005]`

Q6 therefore consolidates Theme 4. The strongest opportunity logic is cross-setting and youth-sensitive: school-linked support, parent education, staff training, and structures that let support reach young people earlier. The presence of 11 `unclear` substantive rows in this question is also methodologically important: the ideas are strong, but some attribution remains incomplete and is therefore kept explicit rather than smoothed over.

### 3.7 Q7: What are your practical suggestions? (ما اقتراحاتكم العملية؟)

**10 participant segments, 5 participant speakers, 2 unclear segments, 3 transcript sources**

Q7 is smaller than the preceding questions, but it remains analytically useful because the suggestions are concrete and youth-facing. The reviewed Day 2 coded base retains direct outward-facing Q7 recommendation evidence in the transcript layer.

> "I think maybe one other suggestion... we can do a social media campaign... and they can trust the services in the school, in the healthcare, to express themselves, and they will get help without being judged." — D2_P21, HWYO9AR, Table 9 `[verbatim transcript, D2_S0313]`

> "Social media can be used through a campaign with explanations on how they can get help, and they can reach out to counselors privately, so they do not feel ashamed." — HWYO9NT1[7], Table 9 `[note-taker summary, D2_E004]`

These Q7 suggestions stay within Theme 4: awareness, private help-seeking routes, and better institutional trust signals. The recommendation workbook remains available as auxiliary material, but it is not required to establish the Day 2 report’s main practical suggestion claims and is not used here as transcript-equivalent evidence.

## 4. Thematic Analysis

### 4.1 Theme 1: Multidimensional wellbeing as balanced stability, moral-spiritual grounding, and daily functioning

**Salience: Most prominent** — 83 coded participant segments, 27 speakers, 6 tables.

This theme is the conceptual foundation of the Day 2 case. Participants repeatedly define youth wellbeing as balance across domains, but they also insist that the balanced life must feel workable from inside: morally grounded, socially supported, and practically livable. The youth case adds a strong functionality component to the wellbeing discussion; being well is tied to coping, maintaining roles, and continuing daily life rather than merely feeling good.

**Why this matters**: The rest of the case depends on this frame. When participants later criticize service delays, stigma, or fragmented care, they are doing so against a prior understanding of what genuine wellbeing should look like.

### 4.2 Theme 2: Youth wellbeing as relational ecology, peer worlds, and social disconnection

**Salience: Moderately prominent** — 32 coded participant segments, 21 speakers, 6 tables.

This theme captures the social setting of the youth case. Participants repeatedly describe youth life through family relationships, peer connections, school life, and the isolating effects of digital overexposure. What emerges is not a simple family-support theme, but a broader relational ecology in which young people may be simultaneously surrounded by others and socially cut off.

**Why this matters**: Day 2 is not only about healthcare delivery. It is about how youth wellbeing is shaped before the clinic and beyond the clinic. This is why education-healthcare collaboration, parental teaching, and social visibility recur so strongly in the later implementation material.

### 4.3 Theme 3: Holistic care constrained by rushed, fragmented, and trust-sensitive encounters

**Salience: Highly prominent** — 44 coded participant segments, 22 speakers, 6 tables.

This theme is the central critique of current care conditions. The recurring barriers are not isolated complaints. They form a patterned account of why youth-supportive care remains difficult: little time, long waiting, weak disclosure safety, cultural and language gaps, and systems that still privilege the presenting physical complaint over the person’s broader life context.

**Why this matters**: Theme 3 is where the report’s deepest tension sits. Participants know what good youth wellbeing support should look like, but they repeatedly describe systems that make that kind of care fragile or exceptional.

### 4.4 Theme 4: Coordinated, youth-sensitive, and institutionally backed routes to change

**Salience: Present but less prominent** — 40 coded participant segments, 18 speakers, 5 tables.

Theme 4 captures the most concrete and future-facing part of the case. Participants call for school-linked support, staff training, parent-focused education, dedicated counsellor roles, social-media campaigns, staff wellbeing infrastructure, and stronger leadership response. The common logic is institutional: change must be embedded in structures, roles, and expectations rather than left to individual goodwill.

**Why this matters**: This theme shows that Day 2 is not only diagnostic. Participants articulate practical, system-facing routes for making youth-supportive care more real.

### 4.5 Cross-cutting pattern: Hidden distress and the unreliability of surface functioning

Across the data, participants warn that what appears fine may not be fine. Distress may stay hidden, be translated into bodily complaint, or remain invisible behind everyday functioning. This pattern runs through Theme 2 because youth struggles are often socially hidden, and through Theme 3 because pressured systems are poorly equipped to notice what people do not or cannot say directly.

---

## 5. Tensions and Contradictions

1. **Ideal vs. service reality**: Participants describe wellbeing in broad, humane, relational terms, then describe care systems that make those same terms difficult to deliver.
2. **Support vs. disclosure difficulty**: Social support is central to wellbeing, yet stigma, privacy concerns, and cultural awkwardness make honest disclosure difficult.
3. **Family as protection vs. family as pressure point**: Family is repeatedly described as foundational, but the same family setting can also be the site of isolation, embarrassment, or hidden distress.
4. **Top-down demand vs. weak follow-through**: Participants want institutional mandates and coordinated implementation, yet they repeatedly describe management systems that are financially or administratively driven rather than quality-led.
5. **Youth focus vs. professional mediation**: The case is about young people, but much of the cleanest evidence still comes from professionals describing youth rather than from sustained youth-led narration.

## 6. Methodological Limitations

- **Source inequality**: `HWYO9AR` and `HWYO0AR` provide cleaner speaker attribution than `HWYO3AR` and `HWYO7AR`, producing uneven participant-level confidence across tables.
- **`HWYO10AR` evidence style**: This transcript behaves partly like compressed summary material, so all quotations from it are explicitly labeled `[note-style transcript summary]`.
- **Unclear-attribution opportunity material**: Q6 and parts of Q7 contain substantive `unclear` rows that remain analytically useful but cannot be counted as participant-diversity evidence.
- **Professional voice dominance**: Although Day 2 includes student and service-recipient visibility, provider and professional voices still dominate the clearest evidence.
- **Arabic register complexity**: Mixed colloquial Arabic, bilingual switching, and source compression mean that interpretive claims depend on contextual reading rather than lexical patterning alone.
- **Note-taker material**: Notes are operationalized explicitly in the outward-facing evidence layer, but only as contextual support. They do not generate matrix or salience counts.
- **Auxiliary recommendation workbook**: The workbook remains available for clearly labeled auxiliary reference only and is not treated as theme-generating evidence.
